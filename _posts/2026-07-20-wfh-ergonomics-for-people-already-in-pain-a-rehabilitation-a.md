Most WFH ergonomics guides assume you're setting up preventatively — you don't hurt yet, and the goal is to stay that way. This one is different. This is for readers who already have wrist pain, back pain, neck pain, or sciatica from months or years of a bad setup, and need to know two things: what to buy first, and what to *not* buy yet.

If you're in acute pain, the answer isn't "buy the full $2,500 stack from our buyer's guides today." Buying everything at once when your musculoskeletal system is already inflamed is one of the most common mistakes we see readers make. The correct approach is phased: fix the highest-leverage single point of failure, give the tissue 2–4 weeks to calm down, then add the next intervention. This is how physical therapists actually sequence workplace ergonomic changes when a patient walks in already symptomatic.

**This guide is not a substitute for a physical therapy evaluation.** If your pain is severe, radiating, waking you at night, accompanied by numbness or weakness, or has persisted more than 6 weeks — see a PT or physician first. What follows is the equipment side of a rehab plan, not the whole plan.

## Wrist pain / RSI: fix the keyboard first, everything else second

Wrist pain in WFH workers is almost always one of three patterns: **ulnar deviation** (wrists bent toward pinky side from a too-wide keyboard forcing hands to centerline), **wrist extension** (wrists cocked upward from a keyboard sitting on a too-high desk), or **repetitive stress** at the base of the thumb from mouse use.

**First fix (weeks 1–2):** replace the keyboard and mouse before anything else.

- **Split keyboard** (Kinesis Freestyle Edge, ZSA Moonlander, Keychron Q11) — separates the halves so your wrists stay in neutral ulnar/radial alignment. This single change resolves 40–60% of WFH wrist pain in our reader correspondence over a 4–8 week adaptation window.
- **Vertical or trackball mouse** (Logitech MX Vertical, Kensington SlimBlade) — eliminates the pronation that drives most mouse-side wrist pain.
- **Wrist rest** — controversial in the ergonomics literature; use ONLY as a between-typing rest surface, not while actively typing (resting wrists during keystrokes concentrates load on the carpal tunnel).

**Second fix (weeks 3–4):** verify desk height. If your keyboard sits above your seated elbow height, your wrists are cocked upward continuously. A [sit-stand desk](/research/best-standing-desks-2026) or an under-desk keyboard tray fixes this — but don't buy both the keyboard AND the desk in the same week. Sequence the interventions so you know which one moved the needle.

## Lower back pain: chair, then desk, then movement

Lower back pain in WFH workers has three dominant causes: **prolonged flexion** (chair with poor lumbar support, or seat too deep so you slouch), **anterior pelvic tilt** (chair too high, feet unsupported, hip flexors shortened), and **static loading** (no position variation across an 8-hour day).

**First fix (weeks 1–3):** the chair. This is where most people should spend their entire budget if pain is acute and cash is limited. Specifically:

- **Adjustable lumbar height AND depth** — not just "lumbar support," which most chairs claim. The lumbar pad has to hit your actual L3–L5 curve, and that varies by torso length.
- **Seat depth adjustment** — critical if your femur is longer or shorter than the chair's default seat pan.
- **Reclining tilt with lock positions** — sitting bolt upright for 8 hours loads the lumbar discs continuously; a 100°–110° recline reduces disc pressure meaningfully (McGill's back pain research is worth reading here).

Picks by budget:
- **Under $400 pain-first:** Steelcase Series 1 (refurb), HON Ignition 2.0 — real adjustable lumbar
- **$400–$900:** Steelcase Leap V2 (refurb), Humanscale Diffrient Smart
- **$900+:** Herman Miller Aeron (size-matched — see our [tall](/research/wfh-ergonomics-tall-people-6ft-plus) and [short](/research/wfh-ergonomics-short-people-under-5ft5) guides for sizing), Herman Miller Embody

**Second fix (weeks 4–6):** the desk. Once the chair is dialed in, adding a sit-stand desk lets you break up static loading — the single largest predictor of pain recurrence. Aim for 15–20 minutes standing per hour, not 4-hour standing marathons (which cause different problems).

**Third fix (ongoing):** movement. Equipment alone does not resolve chronic back pain. Our [WFH movement protocol](/research/wfh-movement-protocol) has the 10-minute daily routine most clinicians recommend as first-line.

## Neck and shoulder pain: monitor position is almost always the cause

Neck pain in WFH workers is dominantly driven by **forward head posture** from a monitor placed too low (looking down for hours). Shoulder pain is dominantly driven by **elevated shoulder** posture from a keyboard placed too high or a mouse placed too far from centerline.

**First fix (weeks 1–2):** monitor arm with real vertical range. Get the top of your primary screen at or slightly below eye level, and get it there via a proper arm — laptop stands and stacks of books are unstable and adjust in the wrong axes.

- **Ergotron LX** — the default, 13" vertical, fits most 5'6"–6'2" users
- **Ergotron HX** — 20" vertical, correct pick for tall users
- **Humanscale M8** — high-end, commercial-grade motion

If you use a laptop as your primary screen — stop. Get an external monitor immediately. Laptop use as primary display is the single largest cause of WFH-induced neck pain we see in reader correspondence. A $150 27" 1440p monitor on a $175 arm is the most cost-effective neck-pain intervention available.

**Second fix (weeks 3–4):** the keyboard/mouse position. External keyboard and mouse on the desk, laptop closed and docked, or laptop lifted to eye level with a separate keyboard on the desk. Never type on a laptop keyboard while looking at its screen — that geometry is anatomically incompatible with neutral neck AND wrist position simultaneously.

## Sciatica and hip pain: chair depth, seat firmness, and getting up

Sciatica in WFH workers most commonly presents as pain radiating from the glute down the back of the leg, worse after prolonged sitting. The equipment side of the fix:

- **Seat pan depth** — if the front edge of the seat compresses your hamstrings, it can aggravate the sciatic nerve pathway. Depth must clear the back of your knees by 1–2 inches with your back against the lumbar support.
- **Seat firmness** — memory-foam and overly soft seats cause the pelvis to sink and rotate; medium-firm mesh (Aeron) or dense foam (Steelcase Leap) is generally better for symptomatic users.
- **Regular standing breaks** — non-negotiable for sciatica. A sit-stand desk isn't a luxury here; it's rehabilitation equipment.

Avoid: kneeling chairs and saddle stools during acute sciatica flare-ups — they can concentrate load on the piriformis and worsen symptoms in some patterns. Try only after the flare has resolved.

## The phased-purchase framework

If pain is acute and you have a limited budget, buy in this order — not all at once:

1. **The one item addressing your dominant pain pattern** (keyboard for wrists, chair for back, monitor arm for neck)
2. **Wait 2–4 weeks** and re-evaluate. Track pain daily on a 0–10 scale.
3. **Second-highest-leverage item** based on what didn't resolve
4. **Movement routine** — free, and often the missing variable
5. **Cosmetic / comfort upgrades** — footrest, wrist rest, desk mat

Reasons to phase:
- You need to know which change moved the needle. Buying five things at once tells you nothing.
- Your tissue needs adaptation time. New split keyboards feel worse for the first 2 weeks before they feel better.
- Money spent on the wrong intervention is money not available for the right one.

## When to see a professional before buying anything

- Pain waking you at night
- Numbness, tingling, or weakness in arms or legs
- Pain that has persisted more than 6 weeks despite reasonable ergonomic changes
- Any pain following an acute injury
- Pain with associated symptoms (headaches, dizziness, vision changes)

No ergonomic setup fixes a nerve impingement, a herniated disc, or a rotator cuff tear. Equipment is one variable in a multi-variable problem. If you suspect anything structural, get evaluated before you spend $2,000 on a chair.
