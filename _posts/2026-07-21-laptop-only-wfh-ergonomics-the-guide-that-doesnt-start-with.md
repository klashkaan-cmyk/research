# Laptop-Only WFH Ergonomics: The Guide That Doesn't Start With "Buy A Monitor"

**TL;DR:** Every ergonomics guide tells you to add an external monitor. Sometimes that's not possible — you rent, you travel, you work from cafés, your desk is 20 inches deep. This is the guide for making laptop-only survivable. It won't be as good as a proper multi-monitor setup, but it can be *good enough* to work all day without pain.

## The core problem with laptop ergonomics

A laptop is one object with two incompatible ergonomic requirements attached to it:

- **Screen wants to be at eye level.** Top of screen at or slightly below eye level, arm's length away.
- **Keyboard wants to be at elbow height.** Elbows at ~90°, forearms parallel to floor.

On a laptop, screen and keyboard are locked to each other. Meet one requirement and you break the other. Meet neither and you get the default: hunched shoulders, forward head, cervical flexion, wrist compression. Meet both = impossible without splitting them apart.

So the entire game is: **decouple screen and keyboard.** Everything below is a variation on that.

## The three viable setups (no external monitor required)

### Setup A: Laptop raised + external keyboard/mouse (best)

Raise the laptop until the screen top is at eye level. Type on an external keyboard and mouse at correct desk height.

- **Screen height:** correct (eye level).
- **Screen distance:** limited by laptop screen size. 13" laptop at arm's length has small text; bump text scaling to 125–150%.
- **Keyboard position:** correct (elbow height).
- **Cost:** $30–$100 (stand + keyboard + mouse).
- **Portability:** medium. Roost/Nexstand stands fold flat. Keyboard/mouse pack in a bag.

**This is the answer for most laptop-only workers.** It replicates 80% of a monitor-based setup with minimal gear. If you take one thing from this post, this is it.

### Setup B: Laptop flat + focus on breaks and posture

If you can't or won't add gear, mitigate rather than fix. Laptop stays on the desk in laptop position. You accept that neither screen nor keyboard is ergonomically ideal, and you actively manage the consequences.

- **Screen closer than usual** (16–20 inches) — worse for eye accommodation, so bump text scaling and take breaks aggressively.
- **Keep the laptop pushed forward** so you're not resting wrists on the laptop edge (that's a nerve-compression risk in the palm).
- **20-minute break rule non-negotiable.** Every 20 minutes, stand up, look far, roll shoulders.
- **Chair height is more important than usual.** Since you can't fix the screen height, at least keep your torso and arms in a good column.

This is a fallback, not a target state. But it's honest — some people rent short-term, work from cafés, or refuse to lug gear around. Manage the mitigation instead of pretending the ergonomics are fine.

### Setup C: Docked at a workstation, mobile the rest of the time

If you split your day between a fixed desk and mobile work (café, couch, kitchen table), commit to Setup A at the fixed desk and Setup B elsewhere. Don't try to make one setup perfect for all locations.

- **Fixed desk:** stand + keyboard + mouse permanently set up. Docking takes 15 seconds.
- **Mobile bag:** just the laptop and possibly a folding stand. No keyboard.
- **Time budget:** limit "mobile" work to under 3 hours at a stretch. Anything longer, migrate to the fixed setup.

The mistake here is trying to build a "portable full setup" — you'll never actually carry it. Two setups matched to two locations wins.

## The gear that actually earns its space

### Laptop stand ($15–$50)

Non-negotiable if you use Setup A. Requirements:

- **Adjustable height** or at least tall enough that YOUR laptop screen top reaches YOUR eye level. Nobody's fits everyone — measure.
- **Stable.** Cheap stands wobble under typing (only relevant if you type on the laptop keyboard while it's raised — you shouldn't be, but many people occasionally will).
- **Ventilated.** Solid-plate stands trap heat on the laptop's underside.
- **Foldable if you travel.** Roost and Nexstand are the reference points here.

Cheap generic aluminum stands work fine for stationary use. Skip the "adjustable" ones with 12 flimsy joints — they collapse. Two or three fixed heights is more reliable than infinite adjustment.

### Compact external keyboard ($30–$100)

For desk use, any 60–75% keyboard works. Priorities:

- **Small footprint** — full-size wastes desk depth.
- **Wireless** (USB dongle or Bluetooth) — one less cable.
- **Comfortable to type on for 6+ hours.** Don't buy something you haven't tried a similar feel to.

Logitech MX Keys / MX Keys Mini and Keychron K3/K7 are common defaults. Anything in the $30–$50 tier from Logitech or Keychron works.

### Mouse or trackball ($20–$60)

Trackpads on external keyboards are usually worse than the laptop's own trackpad. External mouse or trackball is the correct call. Vertical mouse if you have wrist pain (see [vertical mouse guide]). Otherwise any comfortable mouse — Logitech M720, MX Anywhere, etc.

### Optional: portable second monitor ($150–$300)

Not laptop-only anymore, but worth mentioning. USB-C portable monitors (14–16 inches) fold into a laptop bag and give you second-screen for stationary work at coffee shops, hotels, etc. Diminishing returns if your primary "portable" locations don't need dual screens.

## Screen-scaling settings that matter for laptop screens

Small screens punish default settings. Change these:

- **Text scaling: 125% minimum.** macOS: "Larger Text" resolution mode. Windows: Display → Scale → 125%.
- **Font smoothing on.** Both OSes default this correctly, but if you've disabled it, re-enable.
- **Dark mode in the evening.** Reduces overall luminance, easier on eyes at night. Not a productivity claim, an eye-strain claim.
- **Auto-brightness OFF.** Fluctuating brightness fatigues eyes more than a slightly-wrong constant brightness. Set it manually to match ambient.

## Posture rules when laptop is unavoidably below eye level (Setup B)

If Setup A isn't available and you're stuck typing on the laptop as-designed:

- **Distance over height.** Push the laptop as far forward as you can reach the keyboard. Farther screen = less accommodation load; slight forward reach on keyboard is less costly than head-down.
- **Chair up, feet supported.** If you can't raise the screen to you, raise you to the screen. Chair higher, footrest under feet, laptop at maximum comfortable typing distance.
- **Neck resets every 20 minutes.** Chin tucks, ear-to-shoulder stretches. Cervical flexion accumulates; interrupt it deliberately.
- **Bag/backpack posture check twice a day.** Laptop-only workers hunch worse than desk workers. Roll shoulders back, open chest, actively reset.

None of these fix the underlying mismatch. They keep it manageable.

## What NOT to buy

- **Laptop stand + no external keyboard.** You've raised the screen correctly and made typing worse — shoulders pinched up at the raised keyboard. Stands only help if you commit to external input.
- **"Ergonomic" laptop bags with backpack straps.** They help you carry the laptop; they don't help you use it.
- **Wrist rests for laptop keyboards.** The laptop edge itself is the wrist compression point. A wrist rest in front of the laptop just adds to the pile.
- **Blue-light-filter screens taped over laptop displays.** OS-level night mode does the same for free.

## Bottom line

Laptop-only ergonomics is decouple-screen-from-keyboard. Setup A (stand + external keyboard/mouse) is the answer for most people; Setup B is honest mitigation when Setup A isn't possible. Under $100 of gear replicates most of a monitor-based setup. No excuse to just hunch through it.
