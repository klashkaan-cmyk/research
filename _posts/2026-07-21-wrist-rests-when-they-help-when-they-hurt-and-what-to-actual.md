# Wrist Rests: When They Help, When They Hurt, And What To Actually Buy

**TL;DR:** Wrist rests are not for resting your wrists on while typing — that's the fastest way to compress the median nerve and cause the exact pain you were trying to prevent. They're for supporting your **palms** during **pauses**. Below: how to use them correctly, when to skip them entirely, and the specific shapes that match specific problems.

## The core mistake

The name "wrist rest" is misleading and has caused more RSI cases than it's prevented. The most common WFH scenario:

1. User's wrists hurt while typing.
2. User buys a "wrist rest."
3. User rests their wrists on it while typing.
4. User's wrists hurt more within a week.

Reason: **pressing the underside of your wrist into any surface while you type compresses the median nerve at the wrist tunnel**. Static pressure + repetitive movement = classic carpal tunnel provocation. You paid $25 to reproduce the injury mechanism.

The correct use is:

- **Type with your wrists *floating*** — hovering slightly above the desk, forearms parallel to the floor, wrist neutral (not bent up or down).
- **Rest your PALMS (not wrists)** on the rest between typing bursts — when thinking, reading, or paused.

If you can't type with floating wrists for more than 30 seconds without tiring, your problem isn't a missing wrist rest — it's chair height, desk height, or keyboard height. Fix those first.

## When wrist rests genuinely help

Ordered from strongest to weakest case:

**1. Palm rest during pauses (biggest win).** You type in bursts. A well-placed palm rest lets your forearms relax during the between-burst pauses without your hands falling awkwardly. This is what wrist rests are actually for.

**2. Desk edge cutting into wrists (mechanical fix).** If your desk edge is sharp and your forearms rest on it, a padded wrist rest prevents the edge from digging in. Fixing the desk edge (contact strip, tape, softer edge) is better long-term.

**3. Height match on tall keyboards.** Mechanical keyboards can be 40mm tall, forcing your wrists into extension (bent backward) to type. A wrist rest of matching height brings the "base" up to keyboard height so wrists stay neutral. Better fix: low-profile keyboard.

**4. Cold desk surfaces.** If your desk is glass or metal and cold under your palms, a fabric-topped rest is more comfortable. Not ergonomic per se, just pleasant.

## When wrist rests hurt (skip them)

- **You use them WHILE typing.** Guaranteed nerve compression. Bigger problem than the one you're solving.
- **You have any median nerve symptoms** (numbness/tingling in thumb, index, middle finger). Under-wrist pressure will worsen these. See a clinician before more gear.
- **Your keyboard is low-profile** (Apple Magic Keyboard, laptop keyboard, low-profile mechanical). Adding a rest raises your palms above the keys, creating awkward wrist flexion. Wrong direction.
- **You use a split keyboard with tenting.** The tent is the ergonomic feature. A flat wrist rest fights it. If you want palm support with tenting, buy tented palm rests specifically designed for your keyboard.
- **You have a keyboard tray at the correct height.** Tray at correct height + keyboard at correct thickness → wrists are already neutral. Rest is redundant or worse.

## Choosing by shape and firmness

### Firmness

- **Firm foam or gel** (most common): correct default. Enough give to be comfortable, enough support that your palm doesn't sink and force wrist flexion.
- **Soft memory foam:** feels great for 15 minutes, sinks over the day, ends up with palm below wrist = extension = the position you were avoiding. Skip.
- **Hard plastic or bamboo:** correct if desk is soft (padded) OR you specifically want zero compression. Uncomfortable for many people; try before committing.

### Shape

- **Straight flat strip** (Kensington, Fellowes, generic): fine for standard keyboards. Boring, works.
- **Sculpted / contoured with palm dimples:** better ergonomic match. Rest cradles the palm rather than presenting a flat surface. Modest but real improvement.
- **Split into two rests (left + right):** matches split keyboards. Also lets you reposition rests as you move a compact keyboard around the desk.
- **Full-length pads (extend under mouse zone too):** unify keyboard + mouse rest. Good for people whose mouse hand rests on the desk edge. Add clutter otherwise.

### Material / cover

- **Fabric-covered:** more comfortable at long durations, harder to clean, absorbs skin oil.
- **PU leather / synthetic:** wipes clean, feels warmer in summer, cracks eventually.
- **Silicone-topped gel:** cool feel, easy to clean, some people find them "grippy" and prefer them.

## Specific pairings

| You have | Buy |
|---|---|
| Standard TKL/full-size keyboard, no pain | Nothing. Improve chair/desk height first. |
| Mechanical keyboard 30mm+ tall | Firm foam rest matching keyboard height |
| Low-profile keyboard (Apple / Magic / chiclet) | Nothing (don't add wrist rest) |
| Split keyboard | Two matched rests, or tented rests matching your tent angle |
| Median nerve symptoms (numbness/tingling) | Nothing — clinician first |
| Cold glass desk | Fabric palm rest, priority = comfort, not ergo |
| Sharp desk edge cutting wrists | Foam/silicone rest OR desk edge padding OR keyboard tray |

## Setup rules

- **Rest height = keyboard base height.** Rest should meet the front edge of the keyboard so your palms slide from rest to keys without a step. Uneven heights force wrist flexion or extension.
- **Position: under palms, not under wrists.** The heel of your palm should land on the rest. Your wrist crease should be forward of the rest, floating over the keyboard.
- **Rest is for pauses, not typing.** Reinforced by re-reading the top of this post.
- **Same rules for mouse rests.** Palm on rest during pauses, floating wrist during click/scroll.

## Common mistakes

- **Buying a wrist rest for pain caused by chair height.** Chair too low → forearms angle down → wrist extension → pain. No wrist rest fixes this; a chair adjustment does.
- **Wrist rest under a keyboard tray.** You've raised the "floor" your wrists work against, matching the tray. Different variables. Fix tray tilt first.
- **Gel rest for typing hours.** Gel is fine for palm-rest-during-pauses. Not for continuous typing pressure — it warms up, gets sticky, some people react to the plastic.
- **Buying "ergonomic keyboard + wrist rest + mouse rest + arm supports" as a set.** Usually one of them undoes another. Add one intervention at a time; measure the effect over 2 weeks; only add another if the last one clearly helped.
- **Ignoring the underlying position.** Wrist rests optimize a good position. They cannot rescue a bad one. Chair height → desk height → keyboard/mouse position → wrist rest, in that order.

## What NOT to buy

- **"Ergonomic" wrist rests with vibration or heating.** Solving a different problem.
- **Beaded / bumpy wrist rests marketed as "massage."** Creates uneven pressure points under the wrist — worse than a flat surface.
- **Full-desk gel mats.** Trap heat, get grubby, don't do anything specific for wrists.
- **Wrist rests with copper/magnet/"health" claims.** No mechanism. Buy for pad quality, not marketing.

## The 2-week test

Use the rest as described (palms during pauses, floating during typing). Watch for:

- **Reduced end-of-day palm fatigue** → rest is doing its job.
- **New tingling / numbness that wasn't there** → you're resting wrists on it during typing. Stop.
- **Increased ache in shoulders/upper back** → position issue elsewhere; rest is masking, not fixing.
- **No noticeable change either way** → the rest wasn't the missing piece. Look upstream (chair, desk, keyboard height).

## Bottom line

Wrist rests are palm rests for pauses. They are not typing supports. Used correctly, they're a $20 win. Used incorrectly, they cause the exact injury they're marketed to prevent. Fix chair/desk/keyboard position first; add a rest only if palms genuinely fatigue during between-burst pauses.
