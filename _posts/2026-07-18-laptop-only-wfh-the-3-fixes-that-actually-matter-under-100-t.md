# Laptop-Only WFH: The 3 Fixes That Actually Matter

**Working from a laptop all day? You cannot ergonomically use it as designed. Here's what to do instead — for under $100 total.**

If you work from a laptop 8 hours a day, we have some bad news: **the laptop is not an ergonomic device.** It never was. It was designed for short bursts of use on airplanes and couches, and the physics of its design guarantee that anyone using it as their primary work computer will develop neck, shoulder, or wrist pain.

Here's why, in one sentence: **the screen and keyboard are attached, so you cannot have both at the correct height at the same time.** If the screen is at eye level, the keyboard is chest-high. If the keyboard is at elbow height, you're looking down at the screen and grinding your cervical spine every hour.

The good news: fixing this is cheap. Under $100 total, and often under $40. This guide walks you through the three fixes — in the exact order to do them — that eliminate 90% of laptop-only pain without buying a desktop setup.

## Why "just get an external monitor" isn't the answer here

Most ergonomic advice online assumes you're going to build a full desktop setup: external monitor, external keyboard, external mouse, docking station. That's the ideal — and if you can, do it. Our [pillar ergonomics guide](/2026/07/18/the-honest-wfh-ergonomics-setup-guide-2026/) walks through it.

But there are three kinds of readers this doesn't help:

1. **You travel or move workspaces often.** A digital nomad, a hybrid worker, a college student. A permanent monitor setup isn't practical.
2. **You share a small space.** A studio apartment, a shared bedroom. A monitor is too much desk footprint.
3. **You just don't want to spend $500+.** Fair.

If any of those are you, the setup below gets you to about 85% of the pain-reduction benefit of a full desktop rig, for under $100, in a bag you can pack.

## Fix #1: Raise the laptop. Get an external keyboard and mouse. ($30–$70)

**This is the whole game. Nothing else matters until you do this.**

You cannot fix laptop ergonomics without breaking the laptop into two parts: the screen (raised to eye level) and the input devices (at elbow height). You need a separate keyboard and mouse. There is no way around this.

### Step 1: The laptop stand

You need to raise the laptop so the **top of the screen is at eye level** when you sit upright. For most people, that means raising the laptop 5–7 inches above the desk.

Options, in order of portability:

- **$0 — Books.** Stack them. Works fine. Ugly.

- **$30 — Adjustable desktop laptop stand.** Rain Design mStand, Roost stand, or generic equivalents. Not portable but rock-solid. [Adjustable Laptop Stand — affiliate link placeholder]

**Do not skip this because it "looks weird."** Every professional you've ever seen who works from a laptop and doesn't have neck pain is doing this.

### Step 2: The external keyboard

Any external keyboard is better than your laptop keyboard for 8-hour days. Priorities in order:

1. **Comfortable to type on for hours.** Try before you commit if possible.
2. **Wireless, if you move around.** Wired, if you don't (better latency, no battery).
3. **Not RGB-lit gaming nonsense** unless that's your thing. It's distracting for focus work.

Solid picks:

**Wrist rest?** Skip it unless you already have wrist pain. Most wrist rests promote worse hand positioning than none at all.

### Step 3: The mouse

Same rule: any external mouse is better than your laptop trackpad for 8 hours.

**If you have any hand or wrist discomfort already**, buy a vertical mouse. The Logitech MX Vertical ($90) or a cheaper Anker vertical mouse ($25) rotates your forearm to a neutral handshake position. This is the single most under-appreciated purchase in laptop ergonomics.

**If you don't have wrist issues**, a normal mouse is fine. Logitech M185 for $15, or the M720 for $40 with multi-device switching.

## Fix #2: Sit correctly at whatever surface you have ($0–$30)

You've raised the laptop. Now the second problem: **your kitchen table is 29 inches tall, which is too tall for typing.**

### The elbow-at-90° rule

Sit at your work surface. Let your arms hang relaxed. Bend your elbows to 90°. Where do your hands land?

- **On the desk surface** → the desk is the right height. Nothing to fix.
- **Below the desk surface** → the desk is too tall. This is the common case.
- **Above the desk surface** → the desk is too short (rare — usually only kids' desks).

### Fix for "desk is too tall" (almost everyone)

1. **Raise the chair.** Pump it up until your elbows hit 90° at the desk surface.
2. **If your feet no longer touch the floor,** put a box, a stack of books, an old amp, or a footrest under them. Feet dangling in the air is bad for your lower back. Feet flat and knees at ~90° is what you want.
3. **$25 — Adjustable footrest.** If you can't rig something free. Get one that tilts. [Footrest — affiliate link placeholder]

### If you're actually stuck at a kitchen table with a fixed chair

- Sit on a firm cushion to raise yourself, then footrest below.
- Do NOT slump forward to compensate. If you catch yourself craning your neck down, stand up and reset.

## Fix #3: The 30-minute movement rule (free)

Even a perfect setup will hurt you if you sit still in it for 4 hours.

**Every 30 minutes: stand up.** Even for 20 seconds. Walk to the kitchen for water. Do 5 squats. Look out the window. Anything.

This is the single most under-invested-in ergonomic intervention because it's free and requires behavior change, and most people would rather buy a $1,500 chair than remember to stand up.

### Making it stick

- Free timer on your phone. Set to 30 minutes, repeat.
- Kitchen timer on your desk (harder to dismiss than a phone notification).
- Between-meeting bathroom trips count — take the long way.

## What you can skip

- **Blue-light glasses.** Weak evidence. Fix your lighting instead.
- **"Ergonomic" laptop bags.** Marketing. Any padded backpack works.
- **Standing desk converter.** Not a bad purchase, but not necessary for this tier. If you want to stand sometimes, put your laptop on a taller counter for an hour.
- **Wrist rests.** Actively bad for most people — they promote wrist extension.
- **Fancy laptop cooling pads sold as "ergonomic."** They cool the laptop. They do not fix your posture.

## When to graduate to a real desktop setup

You should build a proper desktop rig — external monitor at eye level, laptop as second screen or closed — if:

- You work from the same location every day, and
- You're spending more than 6 hours at the laptop, and
- You have any recurring pain even with the setup above.

## The one-page summary

1. Raise the laptop (foldable stand, or books).
2. External keyboard + mouse (vertical mouse if wrist pain).
3. Sit so elbows are at 90° at your desk. Feet flat on floor or footrest.
4. Stand up every 30 minutes.

That's the entire guide. Under $100. Fits in a laptop sleeve.

If you've been working from a laptop and something aches at the end of the day, one of these fixes is why. Try them in order. Most people feel a meaningful difference within a week.
