If you're reading this at 11pm because your wrists have been aching since Tuesday and every article you've found tells you to "take more breaks" — this one is different. I'm going to tell you the one thing that actually matters (it's not what you think), give you a 20-minute daily protocol you can start tonight, and be honest about when a gadget helps versus when you're wasting money.

## The insight most guides miss: it's not duration, it's position under load

The dominant model in wrist-pain guides is: "you type too much, take breaks." That model is incomplete. The research from Rempel and colleagues at UC Berkeley on keyboard biomechanics — some of the most cited work in the field — points to a subtler picture. Sustained wrist extension under keystroke load raises pressure inside the carpal tunnel. It's not the number of keystrokes. It's the angle your wrist sits at while you're pressing keys.

Translation: someone typing 4 hours a day with wrists flat and neutral will often do better than someone typing 2 hours a day with wrists bent up 20 degrees.

This is why "take more breaks" advice underperforms for a lot of people — it addresses duration but leaves the underlying position broken. You come back from your break, resume the same bent-wrist posture, and the aggravation resumes.

If you take one thing from this post: fix the angle first, then worry about duration.

## Diagnose your pain in 30 seconds

Three patterns cover the vast majority of WFH wrist issues. Match yours:

**Pattern A — Palm/wrist crease pain, sometimes with tingling in thumb/index/middle fingers.** Often worse at night, sometimes wakes you. This is the classic carpal-tunnel-adjacent pattern. Position under load is the primary lever. Do not ignore night tingling — that's the "see a doctor" signal, covered below.

**Pattern B — Sharp pain on the thumb side of the wrist, especially when gripping a mouse or lifting a coffee mug.** This is more likely tendon-based (de Quervain's family). Mouse posture and grip force matter more than keyboard angle here.

**Pattern C — Dull, generalized ache in both wrists that gets worse through the day and eases overnight.** This is usually the "everything is slightly wrong" pattern — desk too high, chair too low, keyboard tilted, mouse too far away. It's the most common and, honestly, the easiest to fix.

The reset protocol below helps all three. The setup fixes further down target them differently.

## The 20-minute daily reset

This is done once a day, ideally at the end of your work session or right before bed. It's not a warmup — it's a decompression protocol. Total time: 20 minutes. No equipment.

**1. Median nerve glides (3 minutes).** Extend one arm straight out, palm up. Bend the wrist back so fingers point at the floor. Slowly tilt your head away from that arm. Hold 2 seconds, release. Ten reps per arm. This mobilizes the nerve that runs through the carpal tunnel. Do not force it — if you get shooting sensations, back off the range.

**2. Wrist flexor stretch (2 minutes).** Arm straight, palm up, use the other hand to gently pull fingers down and back. Hold 30 seconds. Three rounds per side. This is the stretch most people do wrong — it should be uncomfortable but not sharp, and the pull should be at the forearm, not the wrist joint.

**3. Wrist extensor stretch (2 minutes).** Same but arm palm down, pull fingers down and toward you. Same timing. This one is the counterpart most guides skip.

**4. Grip decompression (5 minutes).** Open your hand as wide as you can, spread all fingers, hold for 5 seconds. Then make a loose fist, hold 5 seconds. Repeat for 5 minutes. This is boring. Do it anyway — grip muscles get chronically shortened by mouse use and this reverses it.

**5. Forearm self-massage (8 minutes).** Use your opposite thumb to press into the meaty part of your forearm (both sides) and work slowly from elbow to wrist. Find the tender spots and hold pressure on them for 30 seconds each. This releases the trigger points that refer pain into the wrist.

Do this every day for two weeks before you conclude it isn't working. Most people feel a meaningful difference by day 4-5.

## Setup fixes so you don't need the reset

The reset protocol treats the symptom. Setup fixes the cause. In order of leverage:

**Keyboard height and tilt.** Your wrists should be flat or slightly lower than your elbows, never higher. If your keyboard has flip-out feet that tilt the back edge up — retract them. That tilt was designed for typewriters and it's actively hostile to your carpal tunnel. If your desk is too high to get your wrists neutral, a keyboard tray is the answer. I cover the full seated setup order in [the honest setup guide](/research/2026/07/18/wfh-ergonomics-setup-guide.html).

**Mouse position and grip.** The mouse should be at the same height as the keyboard and close enough that your elbow stays roughly at your side. Reaching forward for the mouse is a wrist killer. If you're gripping the mouse tightly (Pattern B pain), you're probably palming a mouse that's too small — vertical mice or larger ergonomic mice help, but even just consciously relaxing your grip helps within a week.

**Wrist rests: mostly a trap.** Most wrist rests get used wrong — people rest their wrists ON them while typing, which pins the wrist and increases pressure. They're meant to be a landing pad between typing bursts, not a permanent hand-holder. If you're going to use one, use it during pauses, not during keystrokes.

**Standing desk position.** If you use a standing desk, the same rule applies: wrists flat or slightly lower than elbows. Standing does not exempt you from wrist geometry. I cover height calibration in [the standing desk buying guide](/research/2026/07/18/standing-desk-buying-guide.html).

**Laptop-only setups.** If you're working from a laptop without a separate keyboard, you have a bigger problem: laptop geometry forces either bent wrists or a hunched neck, you can't get both right. The fix is genuinely under $100 and I've written it up in [the laptop-only fix post](/research/2026/07/18/laptop-only-wfh.html) — TL;DR: laptop stand plus external keyboard is non-negotiable.

## When to see a doctor (not optional)

The protocol above is for garden-variety WFH wrist ache. See a doctor if:

- You get tingling or numbness that wakes you at night
- You've lost grip strength (dropping cups, struggling with jar lids)
- Pain is severe enough that you're changing how you use the hand
- Symptoms have been present more than 6 weeks without improvement
- You have any visible swelling on the wrist

Carpal tunnel that's caught early responds well to conservative treatment. Carpal tunnel that's been ignored for a year sometimes needs surgery. The economics of "just push through" are bad here.

## Gear that helps (honest, no affiliate placeholders)

Because I don't have affiliate accounts set up yet, this section is intentionally category-only — no links, no specific SKUs pushed. You'll do better googling reviews than trusting a bought recommendation anyway.

**Actually worth money:** Keyboard tray (adjustable), vertical mouse if you have thumb-side pain, split keyboard if you have both-wrist pain and heavy typing volume.

**Sometimes worth money:** Wrist rests (only if you'll use them correctly — as pause landing pads), grip strengtheners (only if pain is fully resolved and you're building back).

**Rarely worth money:** Compression gloves (mostly placebo for typing pain), copper wristbands (fully placebo), most "ergonomic" mice that are just normal mice with a curved shell.

**Actively bad:** Wrist braces used while working. Braces immobilize the joint, which weakens the surrounding muscles and often makes the underlying problem worse over time. Braces are for nighttime, or for acute flare-ups — not for typing.

## The honest bottom line

If your wrists hurt at the end of every workday, you have two problems: your setup is putting your wrists in a bad position, and your soft tissue has adapted to that position. The reset protocol fixes the tissue. The setup fixes fix the position. Both matter. Doing only one is why most people plateau after a week of "trying stretches."

Give it two weeks — the full protocol daily, plus at least the keyboard-height fix. If nothing has changed by day 14, or if any red-flag symptoms above show up, escalate to a professional.
