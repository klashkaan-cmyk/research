If you're 6'0" or taller and your back, neck, or wrists hurt after a WFH day, the problem probably isn't your posture. It's that virtually every mass-market desk, chair, and monitor arm is engineered for a user in the 5'4"–5'10" range — the anthropometric median that furniture manufacturers optimize for because it covers the fattest part of the bell curve.

Once you're above that band, the standard-sized trap kicks in: the desk is too low, the chair's seat depth cuts into the back of your knees, the monitor arm can't lift high enough, and the keyboard tray forces your shoulders into a shrug. You compensate with slouching, forward head posture, and elevated shoulders — the exact positions ergonomics research (Cornell HFE lab, NIOSH musculoskeletal reviews) identifies as primary drivers of chronic WFH pain.

This guide is for the 6'0"–6'8" segment. If you're under 6'0", our general [buyer's guides](/research) will cover you. If you're over 6'8", most of the "tall-friendly" gear below will still fall short — you're in bespoke territory and should consider custom desks and Herman Miller's tall-size chair variants specifically.

## The desk-height problem

Neutral wrist and elbow position for typing requires your elbows at ~90° with forearms parallel to the floor. That elbow height, measured from the floor while seated with feet flat and thighs parallel to the ground, is roughly:

- 5'8" user: 26.5" desk height
- 6'0" user: 28.5" desk height
- 6'4" user: 30.5" desk height
- 6'8" user: 32" desk height

Standard fixed-height desks ship at **29"** — fine for a 5'10" user, forcing anyone taller into a shrug or a slouch. This is the single biggest ergonomic issue for tall WFH workers and the one most people don't diagnose because "the desk is a desk."

**What to buy:** a sit-stand desk with a height range that goes at least 2" above your calculated seated height (for headroom) and 4"+ above that when standing. Uplift V2 (25.3"–50.9") and Fully Jarvis (24.5"–50") cover 6'0"–6'6" cleanly. For 6'6"+, the Uplift Commercial V2 goes to 51.4" and has stronger crossbars for the extra leverage at height. See our [full standing desk guide](/research/best-standing-desks-2026) for the frame-strength math — tall users need Class 3 dual-motor frames because the moment arm at max height amplifies wobble non-linearly.

## Monitor position: the arm-range trap

Neutral neck position puts the top of your screen at or slightly below eye level. For a 6'2" seated user, eye level is roughly 47"–49" from the floor. If your desk is at 30" and your monitor's stand adds 4" to the bottom of the panel, you need another 13"–15" of vertical adjustment from the arm just to hit neutral for a 27" display.

Most budget monitor arms (AmazonBasics, VIVO under $80) max out at 13" of vertical travel from the desk. Ergotron LX gives 13" cleanly. Ergotron HX and the Humanscale M8 give 20"+ and are the correct pick for tall users. Ergotron's own installation guide notes the LX is "optimized for users 5'2"–6'2" seated" — read that as: if you're taller, buy up.

**Rule of thumb:** if your seated eye level is over 46", spec a monitor arm with 18"+ of vertical range and verify the pole/post height on the mount, not just the arm travel. Our [monitor arm decision guide](/research/monitor-arm-buyers-guide) has the compatibility matrix.

## Chair: seat depth is the hidden killer

Tall users almost always focus on seat height and miss the depth issue. Standard office chairs ship with a **17"–18" seat pan depth**. For anyone with a femur longer than ~18.5" (typical at 6'0"+), that means the front edge of the seat cuts into the back of your knees, restricting circulation and forcing you to either perch forward (losing lumbar support) or slouch (losing thoracic support).

**What to look for:**

- Seat depth adjustment of at least 2" of forward travel
- Seat pan depth of 19"+ at maximum extension
- Backrest tall enough to support your shoulder blades (18"+ backrest height above the seat pan)
- Seat height range that goes to at least 21" (for a 6'4" user with 30" desk)

Chairs that pass this test for the 6'0"–6'6" range: Herman Miller Aeron **Size C** (not the default B — this is the single most common tall-user mistake), Steelcase Leap V2 with the tall backrest option, Haworth Fern Tall, Humanscale Freedom with the headrest and 19" seat pan. Avoid Herman Miller Embody for tall users — its fixed seat depth is 15.75" and no adjustment is available.

## Keyboard and mouse: don't skip the split

At 6'0"+, your shoulder width forces standard keyboards into internal rotation at the shoulders and ulnar deviation at the wrists — the classic "hands drawn toward centerline" position that predicts wrist tendinopathy over 12–18 months of full-time WFH.

Split keyboards (Kinesis Freestyle Edge, ZSA Moonlander, Keychron Q11) let you separate the halves to shoulder width, restoring neutral shoulder and wrist alignment. For tall users this isn't a nice-to-have — it's the single highest-ROI ergonomic upgrade after desk height. ## The tall-user starter stack ($1,400–$2,800)

Minimum-viable, no-regret build for 6'0"–6'4":

| Item | Pick | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Desk | Uplift V2 dual-motor, 30"×60" | Goes to 50.9", stable at height |
| Chair | Aeron Size C or Steelcase Leap V2 tall | Correct seat depth, 21"+ seat height |
| Monitor arm | Ergotron HX single | 20" vertical range |
| Keyboard | Kinesis Freestyle Edge with 20" cable | Adjustable split to shoulder width |
| Mouse | Logitech MX Master 3S | Contour supports larger hands |

For 6'4"–6'8", swap the desk to the Uplift Commercial V2 and the chair to a Haworth Fern Tall or a custom-order Steelcase Leap with the tall backrest and extended cylinder.

## What to skip

- **"Tall-friendly" marketing on chairs under $400** — the mechanism required for real 19"+ seat depth adjustment costs more than a $400 chair's entire BOM. If it's cheap and claims to be tall-friendly, verify the adjustment range spec sheet, not the marketing copy.
- **Fixed-height desks with risers** — a $60 monitor riser on a 29" desk doesn't fix the keyboard height. You need a proper sit-stand frame.
- **Kneeling chairs and saddle stools as primary seating for tall users** — they concentrate load on the shins/pelvis at a longer moment arm and accelerate discomfort; fine as a rotation option, not as primary.

## When pain persists after a proper setup

If you've built the stack above and still have persistent neck, back, or wrist pain after 30 days, the problem is likely movement pattern, not equipment. Standard PT referrals for WFH-related musculoskeletal pain in the tall population most commonly diagnose thoracic hypomobility and hip flexor tightness from prolonged sitting.
