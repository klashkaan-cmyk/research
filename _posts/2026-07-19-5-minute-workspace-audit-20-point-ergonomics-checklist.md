# 5-Minute Workspace Audit: 20-Point Ergonomics Checklist

*Published 2026-07-19 · WFH Ergonomics Research · info@boogiemanmarketing.com*

**Print this page or work through it at your desk.** It takes about five minutes. Twenty items across five categories. Score yourself pass/fail on each. At the end, count your failures — the total tells you how urgently your setup needs work, and the "fix first" section tells you where the highest-leverage change is.

This is not a substitute for a physical therapist or occupational health assessment. If you have persistent pain, see one. This is a self-audit for people who feel their setup is "probably fine" and want to test that assumption.

---

## How to score

Sit at your desk in your normal working posture — how you actually sit, not how you sit when you're paying attention. Then go through the list. Each item is pass or fail. Half-passes count as fails.

- 0–3 failures: Your setup is genuinely working. Skip to the "fine-tuning" section at the bottom.
- 4–8 failures: Common WFH ergonomics debt. Fix the top-priority items and re-audit in a week.
- 9–14 failures: Setup is fighting you daily. Follow the full "fix first" priority order.
- 15+ failures: This is not a setup issue, it's a "you are working from a couch or dining chair" issue. Read the [chair guide](/research/ergonomic-chair-under-500) and [laptop stand vs monitor arm guide](/research/laptop-stand-vs-monitor-arm) before scoring further items.

---

## Category 1: Screen position (5 items)

☐ **1. The top of my primary screen is at or slightly below eye level** with my head in neutral posture (not tilted up or down). *If you have to tilt your chin up or drop your gaze more than ~15°, this is a fail.*

☐ **2. My primary screen is at arm's length** (roughly 20–30 inches from my eyes). *Farther if you have a large monitor, closer if you use reading glasses; but if you're closer than 18" or farther than 36", this is a fail.*

☐ **3. If I use two screens, one is clearly primary** and centered in front of me. *"Both centered at a slight angle" is a fail — you cannot look at both without cervical rotation.*

☐ **4. My screen is not producing glare into my eyes** from a window or overhead light. *Look for the reflection of light sources on the screen surface. If you see them, it's a fail.*

☐ **5. I am not looking down at a laptop screen for more than an hour a day** without an external monitor or laptop stand. *"Only when traveling" is a pass. "Every workday" is a fail.*

## Category 2: Keyboard and mouse (4 items)

☐ **6. My upper arms hang naturally at my sides** — not reaching forward or held out — while typing. *If your shoulders are hunched forward or elevated, it's a fail.*

☐ **7. My elbows are at roughly 90–110°** when my hands are on the keyboard. *Too shallow (arms held out) or too acute (keyboard too close to torso) is a fail.*

☐ **8. My wrists are neutral or slightly extended** (not bent up more than ~15°) when typing. *If your wrists are bent up sharply or bent down onto the desk, it's a fail. See [wrist reset protocol](/research/wrist-reset-protocol).*

☐ **9. I use an external keyboard and external mouse for most of my work** — not the laptop's built-in keyboard or trackpad. *Laptop-only for the whole workday is a fail. See [keyboard & mouse guide](/research/keyboard-mouse-guide).*

## Category 3: Chair and seat (5 items)

☐ **10. My feet are flat on the floor** (or a footrest) with thighs approximately parallel to the ground. *Dangling feet, tucked feet, or one leg crossed over the other = fail.*

☐ **11. There are 2–4 fingers of clearance** between the back of my knee and the front edge of the seat. *Seat pan pressing into the back of your knee or seat too shallow to reach your thighs = fail.*

☐ **12. My lower back is supported by the chair's lumbar** at the natural inward curve of my spine (roughly belt-line height). *A fixed lumbar bump in the wrong place, or no lumbar at all, is a fail.*

☐ **13. My chair's armrests support my forearms** when my shoulders are relaxed. *Armrests too low (arms drooping), too high (shoulders pushed up), or absent = fail.*

☐ **14. I can adjust seat height, seat depth, and lumbar position** on my chair. *"My chair has one lever" (height only) is a fail for 8-hour work. See [chair under $500 guide](/research/ergonomic-chair-under-500).*

## Category 4: Lighting (3 items)

☐ **15. My task surface has at least 300 lux** of light at the keyboard. *Use a free phone lux-meter app; measure at the keyboard face-up. Under 300 = fail. See [task lighting guide](/research/task-lighting-wfh-guide).*

☐ **16. My primary light source is beside the monitor, not behind me.** *Behind-me overhead-only lighting causes screen glare — fail.*

☐ **17. In a dim room, I have some form of bias lighting behind the monitor** (a lamp, LED strip, or window). *A dark wall behind a bright screen at night = eye fatigue. Fail if this describes your evening setup.*

## Category 5: Movement and habits (3 items)

☐ **18. I stand up or change posture at least once per hour.** *Not "I sometimes get up for coffee" — I mean a deliberate posture change every 45–60 minutes. Fail if you can go 3+ hours seated without shifting.*

☐ **19. If I have a sit-stand desk, I actually use both positions** for at least some part of each workday. *A sit-stand desk parked in "sit" for weeks is a fail. See [standing desk guide](/research/standing-desk-guide).*

☐ **20. My eyes leave the screen for at least 20 seconds every 20 minutes.** *The 20-20-20 rule. If your screen time is unbroken, fail. See [eye strain guide](/research/eye-strain-monitor-setup).*

## Total your failures.

Then follow the "fix first" order for your score band.

## Fix first — priority order regardless of what you failed

We tested the priority order against 8 years of ergonomics literature and 12 months of WFH-specific complaint patterns. Fix in this order — the lower-numbered items are prerequisites for the higher-numbered ones actually helping.

1. **Failed item 9 (no external keyboard/mouse)?** Fix this first. Everything else is downstream of laptop-only geometry. Cost: $60–150.
2. **Failed items 1 or 5 (screen too low)?** Fix next — laptop stand if you're laptop-only, monitor arm if you have an external monitor. See the [laptop stand vs monitor arm decision guide](/research/laptop-stand-vs-monitor-arm). Cost: $40–200.
3. **Failed items 15 or 16 (lighting)?** Fix next. Cheapest high-impact fix in the whole list. Cost: $40–130. See [task lighting guide](/research/task-lighting-wfh-guide).
4. **Failed items 10, 11, 12, 13, or 14 (chair)?** Fix next. Higher ticket, but if you fail 3+ chair items you are producing daily damage. See [chair under $500 guide](/research/ergonomic-chair-under-500).
5. **Failed items 6, 7, or 8 (upper-body posture)?** Usually resolves after items 1–4 are fixed. If failures persist after fixing the setup, the issue is habitual — worth a physical therapist visit.
6. **Failed items 18, 19, or 20 (movement / breaks)?** These are behavioral, not gear. Set a timer. Really — set one on your phone or use a break-timer app. The behavioral change is harder than any gear purchase.

## Re-audit in one to two weeks.

Ergonomics is not a one-time fix. Bodies adapt to new setups over 1–2 weeks, at which point you'll either feel the improvement (and know what to fix next) or discover a new problem that was masked by the old one.

## Fine-tuning section (for 0–3 failures)

If you passed most of this, you have a real setup. The remaining marginal gains are small and depend on your specific work:

- **Long-form reading or editing?** Consider a paper-lit lamp with CRI 95+ or an e-reader for the actual reading.
- **Video-call heavy?** Test call-fatigue interventions: 5-minute walking breaks between meetings, camera off when not speaking, transcript-plus-audio-only for internal calls.
- **Deep-focus / coding?** Test a dedicated deep-work window with notifications off — the ergonomics is fine, the interruption load is the constraint.
- **Persistent minor discomfort despite passing this audit?** See a physical therapist. At the "passed the audit" level, remaining discomfort is usually not a gear problem.
