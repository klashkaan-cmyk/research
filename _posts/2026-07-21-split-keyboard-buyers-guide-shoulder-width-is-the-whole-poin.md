# Split Keyboard Buyer's Guide: Shoulder-Width Is the Whole Point

**TL;DR:** A split keyboard fixes upper-back and shoulder strain by letting your hands sit at shoulder width instead of squeezed together. That's the mechanism. If your pain is in the wrists or fingers, split alone won't help — you need tenting, tilt, or key-layout changes on top. Below: how to pick, and how to survive the first two weeks.

## What "split" actually fixes

Type on a regular keyboard right now. Look at your shoulders. They're rolled slightly forward and your elbows are pulled toward your body — because your hands are 6–8 inches apart on the keyboard, but your shoulders are 16–20 inches apart. Your upper back is compensating all day.

A split keyboard puts the two halves shoulder-width apart. Elbows drop to your sides, shoulders roll back to neutral, upper trapezius stops holding a static contraction. That's the entire ergonomic case.

Two secondary effects most people don't realize until they try one:

1. **Ulnar deviation drops** — your wrists stop bending outward toward the pinky side. Real relief for people with pinky-side wrist pain.
2. **Neutral forearm rotation is possible** — with tenting (halves angled up in the middle), your forearms stop being forced flat/pronated. Same mechanism as the vertical mouse, applied to typing.

If your pain is genuinely in the upper back, shoulders, or ulnar side of the wrist, a split keyboard is the right tool. If your pain is thumb-base, fingertips, or carpal-tunnel-symptom-shaped, split doesn't directly address it — you may need it AND something else.

## The three categories

There is a bewildering variety of split keyboards. Simplify to three shapes:

### 1. Adjustable split (Kinesis Freestyle 2, Logitech Ergo K860, Cloud Nine, Perixx PERIBOARD)

Two halves connected by a cable, or a single unit with a fixed split in the middle. You place them shoulder-width or slightly wider. Optional tenting kits raise the inner edges 10–20°.

**Pros:** Layout is normal (staggered QWERTY, standard row). No relearning. Cheapest entry point ($60–$180).
**Cons:** Ergonomics gains are real but modest. You get shoulder-width and maybe tenting; you don't get key-layout improvements.
**Best for:** People trying "split" for the first time, or whose pain is upper-back/shoulder-only.

### 2. Contoured / ergonomic non-mechanical (Kinesis Advantage 2 / 360, Advantage Pro)

Deep concave wells for each hand, columnar (not staggered) layout, thumb clusters. Not technically "split" in the two-piece sense, but ergonomically the same category.

**Pros:** Serious reduction in finger travel, thumb clusters unload the pinkies (which do way too much work on standard layouts — Shift, Ctrl, Enter, Backspace).
**Cons:** $350–$500. Real learning curve (2–4 weeks). Layout is proprietary; you WILL be slow on any other keyboard for a while.
**Best for:** People with confirmed RSI, or heavy typists (writers, programmers) willing to invest in relearning.

### 3. Ortholinear split mechanical (ZSA Moonlander / Voyager, Kinesis Advantage 360 Pro, Ergodox, Keyboardio)

Two truly separate halves connected by cable. Columnar layout (keys in straight columns, not staggered). Fully programmable — you rebuild your layout in software.

**Pros:** Maximum ergonomic customization. You can move any key anywhere, tent aggressively, put common keys under thumbs. Best long-term for serious RSI.
**Cons:** $250–$400. Steep learning curve (4–8 weeks to full speed). Programmability is a rabbit hole — real time investment.
**Best for:** Programmers, writers, and people whose careers depend on typing without pain. Overkill for casual users.

## Which one, honestly

- **Pain in upper back / shoulders, no other symptoms →** Category 1. Ergo K860 or Freestyle 2. Cheap, easy, addresses the mechanism.
- **RSI, wrist pain, tingling, career-critical typing →** Category 2 (Kinesis Advantage 360) if you don't want to program a layout, Category 3 (Moonlander/Voyager) if you do.
- **Curious, no acute pain →** Category 1. Don't spend $400 to find out you don't like split keyboards.
- **Under $100 budget →** Perixx PERIBOARD-535 or similar. Fine starter, no shame in cheap.

## The first two weeks will feel bad

This is the killer. Everyone reports the same thing: typing speed drops 30–50% in the first week. Errors spike. You'll want to plug the old keyboard back in.

**Don't.** Two-week rule:

- **Days 1–4:** Slow. Wrong. Frustrating. Normal. Do not use the old keyboard even for "quick tasks" — you'll never adapt.
- **Days 5–10:** Speed recovering to 60–80% of prior. Errors dropping. Muscle memory forming.
- **Days 11–14:** Approaching prior speed on ordinary text. Complex key combos (Ctrl+Shift+something) still slower.
- **After 2 weeks:** If you're still under 60% of prior speed, the layout might be genuinely wrong for you (columnar too aggressive, thumb cluster hard to reach). Consider a different category.

If you buy a Category 2 or 3 keyboard and don't commit to two weeks, you've wasted the money. This is the single most common failure mode.

## Setup rules

- **Halves at shoulder width, not wider.** Wider than your shoulders puts you back in strain, just in the other direction.
- **Tenting 10–20° max.** More is not better. Aggressive tenting works only if you also drop your wrists (arm rests, sled mouse). Beginners: start flat, add tent later.
- **Halves parallel to your forearms, not parallel to each other.** Your forearms angle inward slightly toward center. Your keyboard halves should follow. This is the "opening" that beginners miss — they park the halves parallel and wonder why it still feels squeezed.
- **Same desk height rules as a normal keyboard.** Elbows at 90°, forearms parallel to floor. Split doesn't change that.

## Common mistakes

- **Keeping the old keyboard as backup for "quick tasks."** Kills adaptation. Put it in a drawer for 2 weeks.
- **Buying tenting kit + split + wrist rest all at once.** You can't tell what's helping. Start with just split, add tenting after 2 weeks if needed, add wrist rest only if you have residual pain.
- **Not remapping thumb keys.** If you buy a Category 2 or 3, the whole point is thumb clusters. Programmers: put Enter, Backspace, and modifier keys on thumbs. Writers: put Space and Shift on opposite thumbs. Leaving them at defaults wastes 30% of the ergonomic gain.
- **Assuming split = mechanical.** Category 1 boards are usually membrane. Mechanical feel is a separate axis. Don't overpay for mechanical if you don't care about feel.

## Bottom line

Split keyboards fix a specific problem (upper-back/shoulder strain from hand-crowding) and, in their advanced forms, become a serious RSI intervention. Pick your category honestly: don't buy $400 of programmable if you have mild shoulder ache, don't buy $80 of basic-split if you have career-threatening RSI. Commit two weeks or don't buy.
