# Small-Space WFH Ergonomics: Setting Up in Under 40 Square Feet

Most WFH ergonomics advice assumes you have a room. If your entire workspace is a corner of a studio, a fold-down surface, or a shared dining table, the same physics still apply — you just have to solve them differently. This guide is for the reader working in under ~40 sq ft (a 5x8 corner, a closet office, a Murphy-style setup) who cannot buy a standing desk or leave the chair out overnight.

## The three constraints that change everything

**1. Vertical stacking beats horizontal spread.** A traditional setup wants width — desk, monitor arm, mouse pad, notebook. In small space you go up: monitor on a wall-mount VESA arm anchored to a stud, keyboard tray under the desk surface, tower shelf above the monitor for reference material. See [Monitor Arms vs Stands: What Actually Matters](./monitor-arms-vs-stands.md) for VESA specifics — for wall mounts, verify the stud spacing before buying; 16" on-center is US-standard but not universal in older buildings.

**2. Everything must break down in under 60 seconds.** If your workspace is also your dining table, the setup/teardown friction determines whether you actually maintain good posture. A keyboard that lives in a drawer beats a mechanical board that lives on the table — because the mechanical will get shoved aside for dinner and you'll end up typing on a laptop with your wrists cocked upward.

**3. Lighting matters more, not less.** Small rooms typically have one overhead fixture and one window. You cannot fix bad lighting with position because you have no position to move to. Budget for a dedicated task light (see [Lighting for WFH](./lighting-guide.md)) — a $40 clamp light on the desk edge will do more for eye strain than a $400 chair.

## The $150 minimum viable small-space stack

| Component | Pick | Cost | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Laptop stand (foldable) | Roost or Nexstand K2 | $65 | Collapses flat, fits in a drawer |
| External keyboard (low-profile) | Logitech K380 or Keychron K3 | $40 | Bluetooth, no cable, thin enough to stash |
| External mouse | Logitech M240 or MX Anywhere 3 | $30 | Travel-sized, works on any surface |
| Task light (clamp) | BenQ ScreenBar Halo (if budget) or generic clamp LED | $15-120 | Clips on/off the desk edge |

Total floor: ~$150. This gets your eyes to screen-top height and your wrists neutral without occupying a single square foot of permanent space.

## What NOT to buy for small-space setups

- **Full-size mechanical keyboards.** They're loud, heavy, and don't stash well. If you love mechanical feel, get a 60% or 65% layout.
- **Cheap "adjustable" laptop stands from Amazon.** Most have a wobble at any height above 4". A wobbling monitor is worse than a flat laptop because you'll unconsciously tense to stabilize it.
- **Standing desk converters.** These need 24"+ of depth and permanent placement. Wrong tool for this constraint. See [Standing Desks: When They're Worth It](./standing-desk-guide.md) — the answer for small-space is almost always "not yet."
- **Ergonomic chairs over $300.** In a small space the chair often has to double as dining/reading seating. A dedicated ergonomic chair that lives at the desk 24/7 is a poor fit. Prioritize a decent $150-250 chair (see [Chairs Under $300](./chairs-under-300.md)) or a good cushion + posture-corrector on whatever chair you already own.

## The wrist-neutral test in a small setup

The single most common small-space failure mode: laptop on a raised stand + typing directly on the laptop keyboard. This forces you to reach up and forward, which is worse than the flat-laptop starting point.

**Rule:** if the laptop is raised, the keyboard is NOT the laptop's. External keyboard at elbow height, laptop screen at eye height. Non-negotiable. If you can't fit both, keep the laptop flat and accept the neck compromise until you can.

See [The Wrist-Neutral Self-Audit](./wrist-neutral-self-audit.md) for the 90-second test — it works identically in a small space.

## Weekly reset

Small-space setups drift because the workspace shares purpose. Every Sunday, 5 minutes:
- Keyboard/mouse back to designated drawer or shelf
- Cables coiled (not tangled — coiled cables last 3-4x longer)
- Task light wiped down (dust on the reflector dims output ~15% in a month)
- Chair cushion flipped or fluffed

## When to graduate

You should stop optimizing small-space and move to a dedicated setup when: (a) you're doing >30 hours/week of desk work, AND (b) you have persistent pain that a proper setup would solve, AND (c) you can carve out a permanent 3'x5' footprint. Until all three are true, small-space done well beats "real office" done half-assed.
