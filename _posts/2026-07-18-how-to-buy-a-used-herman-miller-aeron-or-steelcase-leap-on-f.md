# How to Buy a Used Herman Miller Aeron or Steelcase Leap on Facebook Marketplace

**A field guide to the single best value in ergonomic seating — if you know what to check.**

A new Herman Miller Aeron is $1,795. A used one, in working condition, sells on Facebook Marketplace every week for $250–$450. The chair is the same chair. The only difference is whether you know how to inspect it.

This guide walks you through **exactly what to look for, what to walk away from, and what a fair price is** for the three chairs worth buying used: the Herman Miller Aeron, the Steelcase Leap V2, and the Humanscale Freedom.

We wrote this after watching three friends buy used chairs — two got great deals, one paid $400 for a chair with a broken tilt mechanism that would cost $280 to fix. The difference was a 15-minute inspection.

## Why used ergonomic chairs are the best deal in home office

Three reasons the used market is unusually good here:

1. **These chairs were built for 10–12 year commercial lifecycles.** A 5-year-old Aeron is barely broken in. Herman Miller warranties them for 12 years of 24/7 use.
2. **Corporate liquidations flood the market.** Every time a company downsizes or moves offices, hundreds of high-end chairs hit Marketplace at once, at prices set by liquidators who don't know what they have.
3. **The chairs don't wear out gracefully — they wear out visibly.** Frayed mesh, scratched arms, faded plastic. Cosmetic issues that don't affect function tank the resale price by 50%.

Combined: you can buy a $1,500 chair for $300, and if you know what to inspect, get 10+ more years out of it.

## The three chairs worth buying used

We're deliberately narrowing this. There are other good chairs, but these three have the deepest used markets, the best parts availability, and the most predictable inspection checklist.

### 1. Herman Miller Aeron (Remastered, 2016+)

- **New price:** $1,795
- **Fair used price:** $400–$600 for a 2016+ "Remastered" model in good condition
- **Watch for:** Older "Classic" Aerons (pre-2016) sell for less ($200–$350) and are still excellent chairs, but lack adjustable lumbar and have older mesh. Fine choice at the price.
- **Sizes:** Aeron comes in **A (small), B (medium), C (large)**. B fits most adults 5'4" to 6'2". Sellers rarely know the size — check the back of the chair, under the mesh, for a size sticker. **Never buy without knowing the size.**

### 2. Steelcase Leap V2

- **New price:** $1,200+
- **Fair used price:** $300–$500
- **Why it's underrated:** The Leap has better lumbar support than the Aeron for people with existing lower back issues. Padded (not mesh), which some people strongly prefer, others hate. Try before committing if possible.
- **Watch for:** Leap V1 (older) exists and sells for less, but the V2 has meaningful upgrades. Look for the "LiveBack" branding on the backrest tag.

### 3. Humanscale Freedom (with headrest)

- **New price:** $1,400+
- **Fair used price:** $250–$400
- **Why it's underrated:** Self-adjusting recline (no tilt lever) is either love-it or hate-it. Lightest of the three. Great for smaller frames.
- **Watch for:** The headrest version is worth $100+ more than the non-headrest version — check listing photos carefully.

**Chairs to skip on the used market:** Herman Miller Embody (parts too expensive to fix), any "gaming chair" branded product (universally worse than any of the above), IKEA Markus (fine new, no reason to buy used).

## The 15-minute in-person inspection checklist

Print this. Bring it. Do every step.

### Before you drive

1. **Ask for photos of:** the base (5-star wheels), the cylinder (the vertical post), the back of the chair, and the underside of the seat. If seller refuses, walk away — they're hiding something.
2. **Ask "does it hold height?"** If the seller says "yeah mostly" or "it drifts a little" — the gas cylinder is dead. Replacement is $40 and a 10-minute job, but factor it into your price.
3. **Ask the age.** Herman Miller and Steelcase both stamp manufacture dates. If the seller doesn't know, ask them to look under the seat pan for a sticker.

### On arrival: the sit test

**Sit in the chair for at least 5 minutes before you buy it.** Not 30 seconds. The seller will find this weird. Do it anyway.

1. **Height adjustment.** Pump it all the way up, all the way down, back to your height. Does it hold? Or does it slowly sink over 30 seconds? *Sinking = dead gas cylinder. $40 part, deduct from price.*
2. **Tilt / recline.** Lean back with the tilt unlocked. Does it recline smoothly? Now lock the tilt in three positions. Do all three hold? *Weak/broken tilt = expensive fix, especially on Aeron. Walk away or negotiate hard.*
3. **Tilt tension.** Turn the tension knob (usually under the seat, front). Does it get noticeably stiffer? *Broken tension = major mechanism repair.*
4. **Armrests.** All four adjustments: up/down, forward/back, in/out, pivot. Do they hold position or drift? *Wobbly armrests on Aeron are a known failure — the "arm yoke" cracks. $80 part. Deductible.*
5. **Wheels.** Roll the chair. All 5 wheels spinning freely? Any wobble? *Wheels are $30 for a set of 5, easy DIY replacement.*
6. **Mesh (Aeron) or padding (Leap/Freedom).** Any tears, sagging, or sun-fade? Mesh replacement on an Aeron is $150+ and a real project.
7. **Lumbar support.** Does it adjust up/down (Aeron Remastered, Leap)? Does it stay where you put it?
8. **Cylinder.** Look at the shiny post the seat rides on. Any deep gouges or scratches? *Cosmetic — no impact.* Any leaking oil at the top or bottom? *Cylinder failing — replace.*

### Deal-breakers (walk away no matter the price)

- **Cracked plastic on the base or seat pan.** Structural. Not worth fixing.
- **Broken tilt mechanism on an Aeron.** Repair cost approaches the price of a working used chair.
- **Any smell.** Cigarette smoke, pet urine, mildew. These do not come out of mesh or padding. Ever.
- **Seller can't or won't let you sit in it.** Something is wrong.

## What's a fair price?

Two rules for negotiating:

1. **Every deductible defect comes off the price.** Dead cylinder = -$40. Broken armrest = -$80. Sagging mesh = -$150. Do this out loud with the seller.
2. **Cash, in person, same day.** You have leverage. Marketplace sellers hate hold-and-cancel buyers. Being the person who shows up with cash beats being the person who offers $50 more via message.

Rough fair-price ranges for **fully working** chairs in **decent cosmetic condition**:

| Chair | Bad shape | Working | Excellent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aeron Classic (pre-2016) | $150 | $250 | $350 |
| Aeron Remastered (2016+) | $300 | $450 | $650 |
| Steelcase Leap V2 | $200 | $400 | $550 |
| Humanscale Freedom (w/ headrest) | $200 | $350 | $500 |

If the listing is above the "Excellent" price, message them anyway — most Marketplace sellers negotiate 20–30% off asking.

## Where to look, in order

1. **Facebook Marketplace.** By far the deepest inventory. Search "Aeron", "Steelcase", "Herman Miller", "office chair" separately — sellers use different terms.
2. **Craigslist.** Regional. Often better prices in tech-heavy cities (SF, Seattle, Austin, NYC, Boston) where corporate liquidations are constant.
3. **OfferUp.** Smaller inventory than Marketplace, occasionally better prices.
4. **Local corporate liquidation auctions.** Search "[your city] office furniture liquidation." Enormous volumes at wholesale prices, but you may need to buy pallets.
5. **Refurbished sellers (Crandall Office, Beverly Hills Chairs).** Not cheapest, but every chair is inspected, refurbished, and warrantied. Good middle ground if you don't want to inspect yourself. Expect to pay $500–$700 for a chair you'd get for $350 privately.

Avoid: eBay for anything large. Shipping a chair costs $150+ and defeats the value proposition.

## After you buy: 30 minutes of maintenance that adds years

1. **Vacuum the mesh or padding.** Dust and skin cells wear it out faster than use.
2. **Wipe every plastic surface with a damp microfiber cloth.** Not chemicals — just water.
3. **Check every bolt.** They loosen over years. Snug them; don't overtighten.
4. **Lubricate the height cylinder.** A tiny drop of silicone spray on the visible cylinder makes it feel new.
5. **Replace the wheels.** $30 for a set of 5 rollerblade-style wheels turns any old chair into a floaty, smooth ride. Best single upgrade you can make.

Print the checklist. Bring cash. Sit in it for five minutes. You'll do fine.
