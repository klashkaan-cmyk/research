We publish the framework openly because the two most common reasons readers hesitate to write in are (1) they think they'll get a boilerplate reply that upsells them to premium gear, and (2) they don't know what information to include. Both are solvable by showing you the actual process.

## What we ask you to send

The minimum-viable setup review requires five data points. Missing any of these means our reply will be "can you also send X" before we can help.

1. **Your height, in inches or cm.** This determines desk height, chair sizing, and monitor arm range — the three specs where "one size fits all" recommendations fail most often.
2. **Your current desk height, measured from floor to desk surface.** If you don't know, put a tape measure on the floor and measure. Standard is 29", but standard is wrong for anyone outside the 5'6"–5'10" band.
3. **Your current chair — brand and model if known, or a photo if not.** "An office chair from Amazon" isn't enough; the specific model determines seat depth, lumbar adjustment, and armrest range.
4. **Your monitor situation — laptop only, laptop plus external, one external, two externals, laptop closed and docked. Include monitor size.** This tells us whether the neck-pain root cause is a laptop-as-primary-display issue (very common) or a monitor-arm/height issue.
5. **Any current pain or discomfort — location, severity 0–10, and how long you've had it.** Determines whether we're doing preventative optimization or triage.

Optional but useful: your budget ceiling, whether you can modify the desk (rent restrictions matter), any existing gear you're not attached to and would replace.

## The five-checkpoint diagnostic we run

For every setup we review, we run the same five checkpoints in the same order. This is not a marketing framework — it's the actual sequence.

**Checkpoint 1: Is the monitor at the right height?**

We calculate your seated eye level from your height (roughly 42%–44% of total standing height, adjusted for chair position) and compare to your monitor's top-of-screen height. If it's more than 2" below eye level, this is almost certainly the primary problem and the first fix. If you're using a laptop as your primary display, the answer is always "no" and the fix is either an external monitor or a laptop stand + external keyboard.

**Checkpoint 2: Are your elbows at 90° with forearms parallel to the floor?**

We work this backward from your height and current desk height. If the desk is too high for your body, you're either shrugging your shoulders (upper trap tension, tension headaches) or raising the chair to compensate (feet dangling, hip flexor tightness). If too low, you're slouching to reach the keyboard. Fix is either a sit-stand desk with the correct minimum height, an under-desk keyboard tray, or a footrest depending on which direction the mismatch runs.

**Checkpoint 3: Does the chair actually support your body?**

Three sub-questions:
- Does the seat pan depth clear the back of your knees by 1–2 inches with your back against the lumbar support? If not, you're either sliding forward (losing lumbar) or your legs are unsupported.
- Does the lumbar support hit your actual lumbar (L3–L5)? Many chairs have "lumbar support" that lands at your thoracic spine — useless.
- Do your feet rest flat with knees at 90°–100°? If not, footrest or lower chair.

**Checkpoint 4: Are your wrists and shoulders in neutral position at the keyboard/mouse?**

Wrists straight (not cocked up or bent to either side), shoulders relaxed (not shrugged, not pulled forward reaching for the mouse). If shoulder width forces the standard keyboard into an internal-rotation posture, we recommend a split keyboard or at minimum a tenkeyless to move the mouse closer to centerline.

**Checkpoint 5: What's the movement pattern across the day?**

Static loading is the largest predictor of pain recurrence even after a perfect setup. We ask: do you get up? How often? Do you have the option to stand? If not, the recommendation set expands to include a sit-stand desk or converter, not because your seated setup is wrong but because no seated setup is right for 8 hours continuous.

## How we decide what to recommend

Once the diagnostic is complete, we sequence recommendations by two variables: **pain-relief-per-dollar** (highest first) and **your budget ceiling** (hard constraint).

If you're in acute pain and cash is limited, we recommend ONE item — the highest-leverage single fix for your specific pattern — and tell you to wait 3 weeks before buying anything else. Buying five things at once teaches you nothing about what actually helped.

If you're preventative and have budget, we recommend a proper staged build over 2–3 months, in dependency order (chair before desk before accessories, because chair adjustment determines desk height requirements).

If your budget is under $300 total, we point you to our [$300 stack guide](/research/300-dollar-complete-wfh-ergonomics-fix) and adjust it to your specific measurements. We do NOT recommend items outside your stated budget and then add "but if you can stretch to $2,000..." That's the upsell pattern the ergonomics industry runs and it's why most readers stop reading buyer's guides.

## What we won't do

- **We won't recommend a specific product just because it's the highest-margin affiliate link.** The Aeron is the most-linked-to chair in our catalog because it's the correct answer for a specific user pattern (height 5'6"–6'2", seat depth needs, budget above $900). We recommend the Steelcase Series 1 refurb or HON Ignition 2.0 to readers whose pattern says those are correct, even though the affiliate margin is a fraction.
- **We won't tell you to buy premium gear when a $30 fix would resolve your issue.** The VIVO monitor arm has resolved neck pain for readers who wrote in expecting to spend $500. We say so.
- **We won't diagnose medical conditions.** If your pain pattern suggests nerve involvement (numbness, tingling, radiating pain, weakness) or has persisted more than 6 weeks with reasonable ergonomic changes, we tell you to see a physical therapist or physician before spending money on equipment. See our [rehabilitation-aware guide](/research/wfh-ergonomics-already-in-pain-rehabilitation-guide) for the full framework.
- **We won't promise a specific outcome.** Ergonomic changes take 2–8 weeks to show effect on chronic pain. Anyone promising "buy this and your back pain will be gone next week" is selling you something.

## Response time and format

We reply to setup review emails typically within 24–48 hours. The response is usually 300–800 words: the diagnostic summary (what we saw), the highest-leverage single recommendation with rationale, and the sequence for later upgrades if applicable. We don't send templated "thanks for reaching out, here's our catalog" replies.

If your situation is complex enough to need back-and-forth (rare but happens with multi-monitor setups, home offices shared between two people with different body types, or unusual accessibility needs), we'll say so and set up the exchange.

## When to email vs. when to self-serve

**Email us if:** you're in pain and unsure what to buy first; your body is at the edge of the standard-sized band (under 5'5" or over 6'0"); you have a nonstandard setup (small space, shared office, accessibility needs); you've bought equipment that isn't working and can't figure out why.

**Self-serve if:** you're an average-height user with no pain looking to preventatively optimize — our [main buyer's guides](/research) will cover you cleanly without needing a personalized review.
