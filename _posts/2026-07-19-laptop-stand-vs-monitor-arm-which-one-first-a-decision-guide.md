# Laptop Stand vs. Monitor Arm: Which One First? A Decision Guide

*Published 2026-07-19 · WFH Ergonomics Research · info@boogiemanmarketing.com*

**TL;DR.** They solve different problems. **A monitor arm raises and repositions an external monitor.** **A laptop stand raises the laptop screen so you can use an external keyboard and mouse.** If you have one screen (the laptop), buy the laptop stand — a monitor arm without an external monitor to hold is furniture-shaped nothing. If you have an external monitor, buy the monitor arm first and use the laptop closed as a desktop tower, or open on a stand as a secondary display. This guide walks the four common WFH setups, tells you which piece each one needs, and names real products across price bands so you can stop reading Reddit threads and buy the right thing today.

## The one rule that answers 80% of these questions

The top of your primary screen should sit at or slightly below eye level when you are seated with your head in neutral posture. This is the same rule from ANSI/HFES 100-2007 §5.4 that we cite in the [monitor arm buyer's guide](/research/monitor-arm-buyers-guide) and the [eye strain guide](/research/eye-strain-monitor-setup). "Primary screen" is whichever screen you look at most — that is the one that needs to be at eye level, not both.

Corollary: **any screen that is not at eye level must be a screen you look at rarely.** A laptop screen 8 inches below eye level, used for reference for 5% of your day, is fine. A laptop screen 8 inches below eye level, used as your only screen, is a cervical loading problem that will produce measurable pain within weeks (Hansraj, 2014).

Everything below follows from this rule.

## Setup 1: Laptop only, no external monitor

**Buy: laptop stand + external keyboard + external mouse. Do not buy a monitor arm.**

A monitor arm needs a VESA-mount monitor. Your laptop is not that. The three pieces (stand + keyboard + mouse) are the minimum ergonomic setup for laptop-only work, and they cost less than any single decent monitor arm.

The mechanism: raising the laptop puts the screen at eye level, which forces you to use an external keyboard and mouse (you cannot type on the raised laptop). The external input devices restore the shoulder and wrist angles that a laptop's fused screen-and-keyboard geometry destroys. See our [keyboard & mouse buyer's guide](/research/keyboard-mouse-guide).

**Laptop stand options:**

- **Rain Design mStand (~$60).** Aluminum, fixed height (~5.9"), heavy base. Fits most 13–16" laptops. Ten-year proven design. Weak point: fixed height — works for most people 5'6"–6'2" with a low-to-medium desk; short users may still be looking down slightly.
- **Roost Stand v3 (~$90).** Portable, collapsible, adjustable height (6–12"). Best pick if you also travel with the laptop. Steeper price for what it is, but the adjustment range genuinely fits every torso length.
- **Nexstand K2 / K7 (~$35–$45).** Roost knockoff, foldable, adjustable. Not as sturdy as the Roost but 60% cheaper. Real product, widely reviewed, honest 3-year longevity reports.
- **Twelve South Curve (~$55–$70).** Fixed height, visually clean, aluminum. Same use case as the Rain Design mStand.
- **Amazon Basics Ventilated Laptop Stand (~$25).** Adjustable, plastic-and-mesh, cheap. Fine if budget is the only constraint. Wobbles more than aluminum stands.

**Skip:** Any "cooling pad" marketed as a laptop stand. The tilt angle these produce is too shallow to raise the screen meaningfully, and the fans are solving a problem you probably do not have.

## Setup 2: Laptop + one external monitor

**Buy: monitor arm for the external monitor. Laptop stand is optional but recommended.**

This is the most common WFH setup. The decision hinges on how you use the laptop:

- **Laptop closed as a desktop tower, external monitor is the only screen.** Monitor arm on the external monitor, at eye level. No laptop stand needed. Cheapest and cleanest setup. Requires an external keyboard and mouse and either a USB-C dock or a hub.
- **Laptop open as secondary display.** Monitor arm on the external monitor (primary), laptop stand for the laptop (secondary). Place the laptop stand *below and to the side* of the external monitor — the laptop screen becomes a chat/reference display, not something you stare at for hours.

The monitor arm goes on the primary screen because that is the one that must sit at eye level and needs positional adjustment. See [monitor arm buyer's guide](/research/monitor-arm-buyers-guide) for products (Ergotron LX, Herman Miller Ollin, Jarvis, North Bayou F80, etc.).

**Common mistake:** Buying a dual monitor arm because "I have two screens." One of your screens is a laptop. Laptops do not VESA-mount without a $30 tray adapter, and even with the adapter, the weight and depth of a laptop stress the arm's rated capacity. A single arm + a laptop stand is cheaper, sturdier, and more flexible.

## Setup 3: Laptop + two external monitors

**Buy: dual monitor arm for the externals. Laptop stand for the laptop if you use it as a third display; otherwise laptop closed.**

Three real screens is diminishing-returns territory for most knowledge work, so the honest recommendation is often "close the laptop, use two externals." If you must use the laptop screen actively, put it on a stand *below* the external plane so eye travel is downward-and-back, not sideways-and-up (sideways cervical rotation is worse than short downward glances per Bhatnagar et al., 1985).

**Dual monitor arm picks** are covered in the monitor arm buyer's guide. Verify the arm's *total* weight rating covers both monitors — a common failure mode is a dual arm rated for 2×8lb monitors buckling under 2×15lb monitors.

## Setup 4: Desktop tower + one or more external monitors

**Buy: monitor arm(s). No laptop stand needed (you do not have a laptop in the setup).**

Straightforward. The monitor arm rules from the [monitor arm buyer's guide](/research/monitor-arm-buyers-guide) apply directly.

## The four questions that resolve the remaining edge cases

### "My laptop screen is 16" — is it 'enough' by itself if I put it on a stand?"

Yes, ergonomically, if you use an external keyboard and mouse. The 16" screen size is fine for most single-screen knowledge work. Two caveats:

- Font rendering on a 16" laptop at ~24" viewing distance is measurably harder to read for anyone over ~40 (presbyopia onset). Increase system font size 10–20% before assuming you need a second monitor.
- If you do 3+ hours of spreadsheet or multi-window work daily, an external 27" 1440p monitor is a productivity upgrade regardless of ergonomics.

### "I have a standing desk. Do I still need a monitor arm?"

Yes, arguably more so. Standing changes eye height significantly (2–4" typically) between sitting and standing positions. A fixed monitor stand that was correct while seated will be too low while standing, or vice versa. The monitor arm is what lets the screen follow you between the two postures. See our [standing desk guide](/research/standing-desk-guide) — this is why we treat monitor arm + sit-stand desk as a paired system.

Laptop-stand-and-standing-desk combinations are worse. The laptop stand is fixed height relative to the desk, so it moves with the desk but not with your neck. If you use a standing desk, a monitor arm on the external monitor is the higher-leverage buy, not another laptop stand.

### "I use a MacBook Pro with a Studio Display / Pro Display XDR."

The Studio Display and Pro Display XDR support VESA mount (the Studio Display requires the VESA mount adapter SKU — order it with the display, retrofitting is a $200 exchange fee). Monitor arm applies normally. The MacBook goes in clamshell mode (closed) as a desktop tower, or open on a laptop stand as secondary.

### "I use a curved ultrawide."

The [monitor arm buyer's guide](/research/monitor-arm-buyers-guide) has the specific rules — verify VESA pattern (100×100 is common but not universal on ultrawides), verify weight rating (34"+ ultrawides commonly hit 18–22lb), and verify the arm's horizontal reach can position a wider monitor without the base blocking desk space.

## The build-order that maximizes ergonomic dollar-per-hour

Given a fixed budget and the choice of what to buy first, this is the order we would recommend for a laptop-only worker moving to a real setup:

1. **External keyboard and mouse** ($60–150). Highest per-dollar ergonomic gain. No other purchase matters if you are still typing on the fused laptop keyboard.
2. **Laptop stand** ($35–90). Gets the screen to eye level. Cheap, decisive, immediate impact.
3. **Task lighting** ($40–130). See our [task lighting guide](/research/task-lighting-wfh-guide). Under-lit desks drive computer vision syndrome regardless of monitor setup.
4. **Chair** ($250–500). See our [chair under $500 guide](/research/ergonomic-chair-under-500). The reason this is #4 not #1: a good chair on a bad desk setup is worse than a bad chair on a good desk setup, because a bad desk setup fights you for 8 hours regardless of the chair.
5. **External monitor** ($200–500). Productivity + ergonomics upgrade. The 27" 1440p IPS category (Dell U2723QE, LG 27UP850-W, BenQ PD2705Q) is the honest sweet spot.
6. **Monitor arm** ($150–350). Only after you own an external monitor.
7. **Sit-stand desk** ($400–700). See our [standing desk guide](/research/standing-desk-guide). Highest ticket item, worth it if you take the sit-stand ratios seriously.

Total for the full stack: ~$1300–$2500 depending on picks. Distributed across 6–12 months, this is the setup most WFH knowledge workers should aim for.

**If you can only spend $200 total**, buy: laptop stand ($40) + external keyboard ($60) + external mouse ($40) + task lamp ($40) + $20 buffer. That is the highest-leverage $200 in home ergonomics.

## Common wrong purchases we see repeatedly

- **Buying a monitor arm before owning an external monitor.** Confusion between "laptop stand" and "monitor arm" is the #1 mistake we see. The arm needs a VESA-mount screen to hold.
- **Buying a laptop stand with no plan to buy an external keyboard.** The stand raises the screen but makes the built-in keyboard unreachable — you cannot type. Then people abandon the stand and stay flat-laptop.
- **Buying a "sit-stand desk converter" ($150–$300) instead of a laptop stand + external keyboard.** The converter is a much more expensive way to solve half the problem. Only makes sense if you have a real external monitor and want sit-stand without replacing the desk.
- **Buying a dual monitor arm to hold "laptop + monitor."** Laptop VESA trays exist but stress the arm; a single arm + a laptop stand is the correct pairing.
