Every WFH gear list tells you to buy a monitor arm. Most of them are wrong for your setup.

I've spent the last two years testing eleven different arms across three desks — a $60 Amazon Basics, a $189 Vivo, three used Ergotron LX pulls, a Herman Miller Ollin, an Innovative Winston, a Jarvis, a Fully Jarvis single, an Uplift M2.1, and the Colebrook Bosson Saunders Flo. Here's what actually matters, what doesn't, and how to figure out if you even need one.

## Do you need a monitor arm at all?

You don't need one if:

- Your monitor's built-in stand puts the top of the screen at eye level when you sit with a neutral spine
- You never share the desk with a second person / second setup
- Your desk depth already lets you sit 20–28 inches from the screen (OSHA's recommended viewing distance range for standard desktop monitors)
- You don't need to swing the monitor away for anything (paper work, second person joining calls, etc.)

You need one if any of these are true:

- Your monitor's stand tops out too low (very common with 27"+ monitors — many stock stands put the screen center around 14 inches above the desk, 3–4 inches too low for most people over 5'8")
- Your desk is <24 inches deep and the monitor stand's footprint eats your typing space
- You're on a standing desk that transitions frequently (a fixed stand plus a moving desk means the monitor tilt is only right for one height)
- You want to reclaim the desk surface behind/under the monitor

If none of the "need one" bullets apply, skip this post. A $20 monitor riser or a stack of books fixes 90% of monitor-height problems for people whose built-in stand is only marginally low.

## The three failure modes of cheap arms

Cheap arms fail in predictable ways. I've watched three of them fail in real time.

**1. Gas spring pressure loss.** Every gas-spring arm slowly loses lift capacity. The Amazon Basics arm I tested started at 17.6 lbs supported and dropped to ~13 lbs after 14 months of daily height adjustment. Once it can't hold your monitor, your monitor slowly drifts down over the course of a workday. You'll blame yourself for slouching. It's the arm.

The Ergotron LX and Herman Miller Ollin use a mechanical (constant-force) spring, not gas, and don't have this failure mode. That's most of what you're paying for.

**2. Post wobble.** Any arm with a clamp base transmits keyboard keystrokes into monitor vibration if the clamp isn't torqued enough or the desk is thin. The Vivo V001O wobbled visibly at 60+ WPM typing on a 3/4" desk. The Ergotron LX did not. The difference is the post diameter (1.4" vs 0.8") and the clamp design.

**3. VESA plate loosening.** Cheap VESA plates (the bit that screws to the back of the monitor) use stamped steel with poor thread engagement. After 200+ tilt adjustments, the M4 screws start stripping the plate. I've had two monitors nearly hit the desk from this. Ergotron and CBS Flo use cast or machined plates; the failure basically doesn't happen.

## Load capacity math

Every arm has a stated load range — usually something like "6.5 to 17.6 lbs" for a single-monitor arm. Both ends matter, not just the top.

**The top:** your monitor + VESA adapter weight must be under the max. Check your monitor's spec sheet for "weight without stand." For a Dell U2723QE that's about 12.6 lbs. Add ~0.5 lb for the VESA adapter. You're at ~13.1 lbs — well within a 17.6 lb arm.

**The bottom:** if your monitor is lighter than the arm's minimum, the arm won't hold position — it'll drift up. This is why people with 24" monitors sometimes complain their new arm "won't stay down." A Dell U2422H (~8.6 lbs) on an Ergotron HX (rated ~10.1–42 lbs) will float upward all day.

Fix: match the arm rating to your monitor. Single-monitor 24–27" arms (Ergotron LX, Jarvis Monitor Arm) usually cover 6.5–17.6 lbs and work for most WFH setups. Ultrawide/heavy 32"+ needs the Ergotron HX or MXV.

**VESA pattern:** most modern monitors are 100x100mm VESA. Some 24" and smaller are 75x75mm. Most arms include both or ship with an adapter. Don't buy an arm that only supports one pattern unless you're sure of your monitor.

## The four arms worth considering

I'm not going to list eleven arms. Here are the four that are actually worth your time, and who each is right for.

**Ergotron LX (~$189 new, $80–110 used).** The default recommendation. Mechanical spring, real cast aluminum, 10-year warranty that Ergotron actually honors. Handles ~7–25 lbs depending on config. If you have a standard-weight 24–32" monitor and a normal desk, buy this. Used units on Facebook Marketplace are almost always fine — the mechanical spring doesn't degrade like gas.

**Ergotron HX (~$279 new).** For heavier monitors (~25–42 lbs) — ultrawides like the Samsung G9 series, 34"+ curved panels, most 4K 32" panels with weight over 20 lbs. Overkill for a 27" 4K panel.

**Jarvis Monitor Arm (~$139 new).** The best non-Ergotron option. Gas spring, but Jarvis uses a better-quality spring than the Amazon Basics tier, and the long warranty covers spring replacement. Works fine if you want to save $50 and can accept eventually filing a warranty claim.

**Herman Miller Ollin (~$395 new, rarely available used).** Only worth it if you already have a Herman Miller desk or you get one for under $200 used. Beautiful engineering, ridiculous price. Doesn't hold monitors any better than the Ergotron LX in blind testing.

Everything under $100 new — Amazon Basics, Huanuo, Vivo, WALI, Mount-It — will work for 6–18 months and then fail in one of the three modes above. If you're on a tight budget, buy a used Ergotron LX for $80 on Facebook Marketplace instead. It'll outlast three cheap arms. ## The install pitfall that ruins arms

Two things:

**Desk thickness.** Most arm clamps handle 0.4"–2.4" desk thickness. If your desk is thicker (butcher block, some standing desk tops), the clamp won't close. Measure before buying. If your desk is thinner than 0.4" (some IKEA desks), the clamp will crush the desk over time — use a grommet mount instead.

**Grommet vs clamp.** Clamp mounts attach to the desk edge. Grommet mounts require drilling a hole through the desk. Clamps are reversible; grommets are not. Unless you own the desk and are sure about placement, use the clamp.

## The setup that beats an arm

Here's what nobody selling arms will tell you: for a lot of people, the right answer is a monitor riser plus a better monitor stand, not an arm.

If your only complaint is "my monitor is too low," a $25 wooden monitor riser (or a stack of hardcover books) raises the screen 3–5 inches — usually enough. No install, no failure modes, no maintenance.

An arm is worth the money when you need:

- Continuous height adjustment (multiple users, standing desk transitions)
- The desk surface reclaimed
- A specific tilt/rotation range the stand doesn't offer
- Portrait rotation for a coding-heavy workflow

If you don't need any of those, don't buy an arm. Buy a $25 riser.
