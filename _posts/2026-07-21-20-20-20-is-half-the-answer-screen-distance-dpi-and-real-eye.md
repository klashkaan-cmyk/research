# 20-20-20 Is Half The Answer: Screen Distance, DPI, And Real Eye-Strain Fixes

**TL;DR:** The 20-20-20 rule (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds) is real advice — but it only addresses accommodation fatigue. It does nothing for the other three causes of computer eye strain: screen too close, text too small, and reduced blink rate. Full fix requires all four. No gear needed.

## The four separate problems people conflate as "eye strain"

"My eyes hurt at the end of the day" has four distinct causes. Different fixes for each. Most guides mix them up and prescribe one solution for all.

1. **Accommodation fatigue** — the ciliary muscles inside your eye stay contracted to keep near objects in focus. All day of near-focus = muscle fatigue. → Fix: distance breaks (20-20-20).
2. **Convergence strain** — both eyes rotate inward to focus on a near object. Sustained convergence tires the extraocular muscles. → Fix: distance breaks AND correct screen distance.
3. **Dry eye / reduced blinks** — blink rate drops from ~15/min to ~5/min when reading screens. Corneal surface dries, feels gritty. → Fix: bigger text (fewer squints), better humidity, deliberate blinking.
4. **Squint from text-too-small** — high-DPI displays render text physically small even when it looks "sharp." Users lean in unconsciously. → Fix: increase system text scaling.

If you only apply 20-20-20, you address #1 and partially #2. #3 and #4 remain, and they're bigger contributors for most people.

## Fix #1: Screen distance (the one everyone gets wrong)

Correct viewing distance: **arm's length. Roughly 20–30 inches from your eyes to the screen surface.**

Test right now: extend your arm toward the screen. Your fingertips should touch the screen or come within a couple inches. If your fingers are 8 inches short, the screen is too far (rare). If you can lean your palm on it, way too close (common — laptop on lap or on a low desk).

**Laptop users: this is why your eyes hurt.** Laptop screens are typically 15–20 inches from your face by default posture. Too close, all day. Every hour of laptop-close is extra convergence load. External monitor at arm's length fixes this immediately, more than any break routine.

**Ultrawide users: push it back farther than a normal monitor.** The edges are physically farther from your eyes than the center. If you set an ultrawide at 20 inches, the far edges are 26+ inches away and you're rapidly re-focusing every time you check a corner. Set the center at 28–32 inches instead.

## Fix #2: Text size (bigger than you think)

The "resolution" era ended around 2018. Since then, high-DPI displays (4K, Retina, QHD on small panels) render text physically tiny even though it looks sharp. Users perceive "clear" and "readable" as the same thing. They aren't.

**Fast test:** sit at correct distance. Try to read this article's body text without leaning forward. If you lean, your text is too small. Full stop.

Fixes:

- **macOS:** Settings → Displays → Resolution → "Larger Text" or scaled resolution one step lower than native. On a 4K 27" monitor most people should run "looks like 2560x1440" or "looks like 1920x1080," not native 4K.
- **Windows:** Settings → Display → Scale → 125% or 150% on 4K, 150% on 27"+ QHD.
- **Browser:** Ctrl/Cmd + "+" until reading feels effortless. This isn't a preference — if you're squinting, you're causing strain.
- **Kindle/e-reader for long reading:** e-ink is easier on eyes than any LCD. If your job includes 2+ hours of pure reading, offload it.

The reflex is "but I don't want everything to look big." Yes you do. Squinting all day to keep things "small and elegant" is directly causing the symptoms you're googling about.

## Fix #3: Deliberate blinking + humidity

Blink rate drops when you concentrate on screens. This is neurological, not behavioral — you can't just "remember to blink more." Two workarounds:

- **Full blinks, not partial.** Most on-screen blinks are half-blinks that don't fully spread tear film. Every 10 minutes, do 3–4 deliberate hard blinks. This is stupid-sounding advice that measurably helps.
- **Humidity 40–60%.** Sub-30% office/HVAC air dries corneal surface fast. A $30 room humidifier in winter is a legitimate ergonomic purchase.
- **Preservative-free artificial tears if you're chronically dry.** Not something to use aggressively, but if you're already reaching for eye drops, use PF ones (Systane Ultra PF, Refresh Optive Mega-3, similar). The preservatives in regular drops can worsen dryness with heavy use.

## Fix #4: The actual 20-20-20 protocol

Set a repeating 20-minute timer. Every 20 minutes, look at something 20+ feet away for 20 seconds. That's it.

Practical variants that people actually stick with:

- **Window rule** — every time you finish a task/meeting, look out a window for 20 seconds. Task-boundary is more natural than a timer.
- **Standup rule** — combine with sit-stand alternation. Every time you change position, do the 20-second distant look.
- **Doorway rule** — every time you walk through a doorway, look down the hall. Passive habit stacking; no discipline needed.

Timer-based works for the disciplined; habit-stacked works for everyone else.

## The blue-light glasses question

Evidence for blue-light glasses reducing eye strain is weak. Evidence for them helping sleep (when worn evenings) is moderate. Buy them for evening use if you're using screens after dark and having trouble falling asleep. Don't buy them expecting daytime eye-strain relief — that's not what the research supports.

Screen tinting (macOS Night Shift, Windows Night Light, f.lux) does the same thing for free.

## Ordered priority

If you're going to do only one thing today:

1. **Screen at arm's length.** Free. Biggest impact.
2. **Bump text scaling.** Free. Second biggest.
3. **Habit-stacked distance breaks.** Free.
4. **Room humidifier + deliberate blinks.** $30 + attention.
5. **Blue-light glasses (evenings only).** Optional, sleep benefit only.

Notice what's not on the list: expensive monitors, special "eye-care" software, ergonomic anti-glare films. They're not wrong, they're not primary.

## When to see an actual eye doctor

DIY fixes have limits. See an optometrist/ophthalmologist if:

- Symptoms don't improve after 2 weeks of correct distance + text scaling.
- Blurred vision that lasts more than a few minutes after screen use.
- Headaches concentrated behind the eyes.
- Prescription is >2 years old (uncorrected refractive error is a giant eye-strain source people ignore).
- Persistent dryness/redness. Could be blepharitis or meibomian gland dysfunction — treatable, but not by ergonomics.

## Bottom line

20-20-20 is real but partial. The bigger wins are screen distance (arm's length) and text scaling (larger than you think). Both are free, both take 2 minutes to fix, and both matter more than any product you can buy for eye strain.
