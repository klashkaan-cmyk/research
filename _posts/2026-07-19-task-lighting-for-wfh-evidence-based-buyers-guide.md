# Task Lighting for WFH: Evidence-Based Buyer's Guide

*Published 2026-07-19 · WFH Ergonomics Research · info@boogiemanmarketing.com*

**TL;DR.** Most WFH lighting problems are not about "brightness." They are about three separable variables — **illuminance** (how much light lands on your desk, measured in lux), **correlated color temperature (CCT)** (warm vs cool, measured in Kelvin), and **color rendering index (CRI)** (how accurately colors appear, 0–100). IES RP-1 recommends **300–500 lux** at the task surface for general office work, **500–750 lux** for detailed work like reading small text or fine editing. Your overhead ceiling light almost certainly delivers 100–200 lux at desk height. That's the problem. Fix it with a proper task lamp — not a brighter bulb — because a task lamp lets you control the *direction* of light, which is the actual driver of glare and eye strain. This guide grades the variables by evidence, names real products across price bands, and lists the marketing terms to ignore.

## The three variables that actually matter

### 1. Illuminance (lux at the task surface)

**Evidence: strong.** IES RP-1 (Office Lighting) and EN 12464-1 (European standard for indoor workplace lighting) both recommend:

- **300–500 lux** for general office tasks (typing, video calls, most computer work)
- **500–750 lux** for reading fine print, detailed drawing, proofreading
- **750–1000 lux** for CAD, drafting, precision assembly

A typical residential ceiling fixture delivers **100–200 lux** at desk height. That is a lighting deficit of roughly 3–5×. The consequence is measurable: reduced light triggers pupil dilation, which reduces depth of field, which forces the ciliary muscle to work harder to maintain focus — the mechanism behind computer vision syndrome (Sheedy et al., 2003; Rosenfield, 2016).

**How to measure yours in 60 seconds.** Free apps like "Lux Light Meter" (iOS) or "Lux Meter" (Android) use the phone's ambient sensor. Not lab-grade — typically ±15–25% — but easily accurate enough to tell you if you're at 150 lux vs 400 lux. Measure at your keyboard with the phone face-up.

### 2. Correlated Color Temperature (CCT), measured in Kelvin

**Evidence: moderate for alertness/circadian effects; strong for preference.**

- **2700–3000K** = "warm white" (incandescent-like)
- **3500–4000K** = "neutral white"
- **5000–6500K** = "daylight" / "cool white"

Cooler CCT (higher K) suppresses melatonin more effectively and increases subjective alertness during daytime hours (Cajochen et al., 2011; Chellappa et al., 2011). Warmer CCT reduces melatonin suppression and is preferred in evening hours (Gooley et al., 2011).

**Practical rule:**
- Daytime work: 4000–5000K
- Evening work / winding down: 2700–3000K
- **Best: a tunable lamp that lets you shift CCT across the day.** This maps to circadian evidence — see Blume et al., 2019 for a plain-language review.

### 3. Color Rendering Index (CRI), 0–100

**Evidence: strong for perceived quality; moderate for visual comfort.**

CRI measures how accurately a light source renders colors compared to a reference (sunlight or a black-body radiator). CRI 80 is the minimum for office spec per EN 12464-1. CRI 90+ is meaningfully better and worth the small premium.

**What CRI is not:** brightness. A CRI 95 lamp at 100 lux is still too dim for reading. CRI and illuminance are independent.

**Cheap LEDs commonly have CRI 70–80 with a poor R9 (red saturation) score** — this is why skin looks slightly green under some cheap desk lamps. Look for CRI ≥90 and R9 ≥50 if the spec sheet lists it.

### The variable that matters more than any spec: **direction**

None of the numbers above matter if the light is glaring off your screen. The single largest driver of WFH lighting complaints is *light direction*, not lumens. IES RP-1 §4.4 addresses this directly: task lighting should come from **beside and slightly behind** the plane of the screen, angled *away* from the screen surface, so the reflection off any glossy surface (screen, glossy paper, keyboard) does not enter the eye.

Practical rules:
- Task lamp **beside** the monitor, not behind you (behind you = screen glare)
- Lamp arm long enough to place the shade **above and to the side** of the keyboard
- Shade design should **conceal the LED source** at typical viewing angles — bare LED strips create discomfort glare (measured as UGR, Unified Glare Rating; office spec is UGR ≤19)

## What overhead lighting cannot fix

Even a bright ceiling fixture cannot replace a task lamp because:

1. **Ceiling light is omnidirectional and diffuse.** You cannot aim it at your work surface without also aiming it at your screen.
2. **Ceiling light casts shadows from your head onto your keyboard** — the classic hunched-over-the-keyboard shadow.
3. **You cannot dim/tune it independently** from your work rhythm. A task lamp lets you go 5000K/500 lux at 10am and 3000K/300 lux at 8pm without touching the room lighting.

Upgrading the ceiling bulb is a $15 experiment worth trying (get a 4000K, CRI 90+, 1600-lumen bulb), but it is a *supplement* to task lighting, not a replacement.

## Full-spectrum / light therapy considerations

If you experience seasonal affective symptoms or persistent morning grogginess, a **10,000 lux light therapy box** (Carex Day-Light Classic Plus, Northern Light Technologies Boxelite) has strong evidence for treating SAD and delayed sleep phase (Golden et al., 2005 — American Journal of Psychiatry meta-analysis). This is a **medical intervention, not a task lamp**, and the two should not be conflated:

- Light therapy: 10,000 lux at eye level, 20–30 minutes, morning use
- Task lighting: 300–750 lux at task surface, throughout the workday

Buying a $50 "SAD lamp" that outputs 2,500 lux at 12 inches is not a treatment — the dose-response curve for SAD requires the 10,000 lux threshold at the appropriate distance.

## Lamps and marketing terms we would avoid

- **"Blue light blocking" desk lamps.** No credible evidence blue-light-blocking lighting improves eye strain or sleep at office illuminance levels (see AAO 2023 clinical guidance, and the 2021 Cochrane review Singh et al. on blue-blocking spectacle lenses — same mechanism argument, no benefit found).
- **RGB "gaming" desk lamps** without white-light specs. If the spec sheet does not disclose CCT and CRI for the white mode, treat it as decorative lighting, not task lighting.
- **Bare LED strip lights** (e.g., wrapped around monitor edges) used as primary task lighting. Fine as accent lighting; bad as task lighting because the LED source is visible at direct viewing angles (fails UGR).
- **"Full spectrum" claims without CRI numbers.** "Full spectrum" is unregulated marketing. CRI 90+ / R9 50+ is the actual spec you want.
- **Halogen desk lamps.** Heat output and short bulb life make them a bad fit for 8-hour daily use.

## Setup checklist (do this once, benefit forever)

1. **Measure current lux** at your keyboard with a phone light meter app. If under 300 lux, you have a real problem.
2. **Position task lamp beside the monitor**, on the side opposite your dominant hand (so writing/mousing does not cast a shadow onto the task).
3. **Aim shade so bare LED is not visible** at your normal seated eye height.
4. **Set daytime CCT to 4000–5000K.** Set evening (post ~6pm) to 2700–3000K if your lamp is tunable.
5. **Measure again.** Target 400–500 lux at keyboard for general work.
6. **Add ambient/bias lighting behind the monitor** in dim rooms (a cheap $15 LED strip on the back of the monitor stand). Reduces screen-to-background contrast, which the eye strain literature associates with fatigue reduction.
7. **Kill screen glare.** If your window is behind or in front of the screen, add blinds or reposition. See [eye strain and monitor setup](/research/eye-strain-monitor-setup).

## Cheap wins if you cannot buy a lamp today

- **Upgrade the closest ceiling bulb** to a 1600-lumen, 4000K, CRI 90+ LED (Philips Ultra Definition or GE Reveal HD+). Cost: $8–15.
- **Move your desk within 6 feet of a window.** Daylight is the highest-CRI light source available and free. Blinds control glare.
- **Add a $15 dimmable LED strip behind the monitor** for bias lighting.
- **Turn off any overhead fluorescent** that flickers at 60/120 Hz — subclinical flicker is a documented headache trigger (Wilkins et al., 1989).
