Every WFH ergonomics buyer's guide — including ours — quietly assumes you have $1,500–$3,000 to spend on a proper setup. Most WFH workers do not. If your reality is "I need to fix my back pain and my desk situation for under $300 total, this month, on a real budget," almost none of the mainstream guides speak to you.

This one does. It's not a compromise stack — it's a **priority-of-spend** guide. The core insight: if you already have a table you can work at (a kitchen table, a hollow-core door on filing cabinets, an IKEA Linnmon), you don't need to buy a $600 sit-stand desk to fix 80% of your pain. You need to spend the $300 on the four items that actually move the ergonomic needle, and skip everything else until you have more money.

## Where the premium stack over-charges

The mainstream WFH stack ($2,500) breaks down roughly as:

- $600 sit-stand desk
- $900 chair (Aeron, Leap)
- $400 monitor + $200 arm
- $200 split keyboard
- $100 mouse
- $100 accessories

Where you can cut without losing ergonomic benefit:

- **Desk:** if you have a table at roughly the right height, a $30 monitor riser + a $60 keyboard tray gets you 70% of a sit-stand desk's benefit (correct monitor + keyboard height). You lose the standing option. You do not lose the neutral posture.
- **Chair:** the used office furniture market is enormous. A 5-year-old Steelcase Leap V2 sells for $200–$300 on Facebook Marketplace and OfferUp in most major metros. It is the same chair. It has 10+ years of life left. You're paying $700+ new for the sticker.
- **Monitor:** if you already own one (or have a laptop with a decent screen), the arm matters more than the monitor. A $30 VIVO arm is not as smooth as a $180 Ergotron LX, but it holds a monitor at the right height, which is 90% of the ergonomic benefit.
- **Keyboard/mouse:** the split keyboard is a comfort-and-long-term-injury-prevention upgrade, not a "you must have this to be pain-free" item. A standard keyboard positioned correctly beats a split keyboard positioned wrong.

## What actually matters — the four-item hierarchy

If you have $300 total, spend it in this order until you run out:

**1. Chair adjustability — $150–$200**

This is where the majority of the budget goes. Non-negotiable specs at this price point:

- Adjustable seat height (nearly all chairs have this — don't pay extra for it)
- Adjustable armrest height AND width (many budget chairs skip width — it matters)
- Real lumbar adjustment (not a fixed foam pad — an actual mechanism that moves up/down)
- Recline with tilt lock

Picks that hit this at $150–$300:

- **Used Steelcase Leap V2** — $200–$300 on Facebook Marketplace / OfferUp / used office furniture dealers. Single highest-value chair purchase in this budget. Search "Steelcase Leap V2 used [your city]."
- **HON Ignition 2.0** — $250 new on Amazon. Mesh back, real adjustments, boring but solid.
- **Autonomous ErgoChair Core** — $200 direct. Not as refined as Steelcase but hits all the adjustment specs.

Avoid: any "gaming chair" under $300 (racing-bucket seats are ergonomically terrible for typing posture), any chair without adjustable lumbar HEIGHT.

**2. Monitor position — $30**

A **VIVO V001 single monitor arm** ($30 on Amazon) is not a luxury item. It's the difference between looking down at your monitor for 8 hours a day (guaranteed neck pain within months) and having the top of the screen at eye level (no neck strain). No other $30 item in the WFH ergonomics category moves the needle this much.

If you don't have an external monitor and use a laptop as your primary display: buy a **used 24" or 27" 1080p/1440p monitor** on Facebook Marketplace ($60–$100) before you buy anything else on this list. Laptop-as-primary-display is the #1 driver of WFH neck pain we see. This is worth reshuffling the budget for.

**3. Keyboard tray or riser — $30–$60**

If your existing desk is 29" (standard) and you're under 5'10", your keyboard is too high. You will shrug your shoulders continuously. Two fixes:

- **Under-desk keyboard tray:** ~$60 for a basic one (Mount-It! or VIVO). Drops your typing surface 4"–5" below desk height. Best fix.
- **Lap desk with wrist support:** $30. Not as good, but works if your desk can't take a tray.

If you're over 5'10" and your desk feels too low, this is reversed — you need a desk riser under the monitor and keyboard both, or a taller desk. A stack of textbooks under your existing desk legs works surprisingly well and costs $0.

**4. Footrest — $25–$50**

If your chair is high enough for your elbows to reach the desk but your feet don't reach the floor, you need a footrest. This is dominantly a short-user issue but affects anyone under 5'8" on a 29" desk without a keyboard tray. **ErgoFoam Adjustable** ($40 on Amazon) is fine — the premium Humanscale FR300 ($180) is not necessary at this budget.

If you don't need a footrest (feet reach the floor comfortably), reallocate this $25–$50 to a better chair or a keyboard tray.

## The $500 upgrade path

If you can stretch to $500, add in this order:

1. **Better monitor arm** ($100–$180 Ergotron LX) — smoother adjustment, holds position, lasts 10+ years
2. **Better keyboard** ($90 Keychron K8 TKL) — mouse closer to centerline, real mechanical feel
3. **Sit-stand desk converter** ($150 Vari or Flexispot) — lets you stand without buying a whole new desk

If you can stretch to $800, add a used Ergotron LX ($60 refurb), a Kinesis Freestyle Edge split keyboard ($200 refurb), and a proper monitor if you don't have one.

## What NOT to buy at this price

- **Gaming chairs** — the ergonomic story on these is bad. Fixed-angle backrests, poor lumbar mechanisms, seat pans designed for a very specific body position. Skip regardless of budget.
- **Standing desk converters under $80** — they wobble, they don't hold height, they drop your keyboard 2 weeks in.
- **"Ergonomic" foam wrist rests marketed as anti-RSI** — they help as a between-typing surface, they do not prevent RSI if you're actively typing on them.
- **Cheap knock-off monitor arms under $20** — they sag, gas cylinders fail in 6 months, monitor drifts down. VIVO V001 at $30 is the price floor for something that works.
- **"Ergonomic" mice under $15** — the shape matters, the click feel matters, and the sub-$15 tier fails on both.

## When to save up vs. buy now

**Buy now, at $300:** if you're in acute pain, if you're working full-time from a bad setup, if you've been putting this off for months.

**Save up to $600:** if you're preventatively upgrading, if you have flexibility, if you can wait 2–3 months. The $600 stack (add a proper sit-stand converter and a Keychron K8) is meaningfully better and doesn't cost 2× — it costs 2× but lasts 3× longer.

**Save up to $1,500:** if you plan to WFH indefinitely, if this is your primary workspace for the next 5+ years. See our [main buyer's guides](/research) for the full stack.

Don't wait to fix pain just because you can't afford the premium stack. The used-Steelcase-plus-VIVO-arm-plus-keyboard-tray combo has resolved chronic pain for readers who wrote in expecting they needed $2,000 to fix it. They didn't.
