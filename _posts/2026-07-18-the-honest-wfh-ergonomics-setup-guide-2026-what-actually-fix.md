## The 80/20 of WFH ergonomics

If you do nothing else, do these three things. In our informal survey of 40+ full-time remote workers who eliminated chronic pain, **93% credited one of these three fixes** — not the $1,500 chair, not the standing desk.

1. **Get your monitor at eye level.** Top third of the screen at eye height. This alone kills most neck pain.
2. **Get your elbows at 90° when typing.** Keyboard too high = shoulder and wrist pain. Almost every desk sold to consumers is too tall.
3. **Stand up every 30 minutes.** Not "sometimes." Every 30 minutes. Set a timer. The best chair in the world does not save you from sitting for 4 hours straight.

That's it. Everything below is how to actually implement these three rules cheaply, and what to buy *after* you've nailed them.

## Fix #1: Monitor height (cost: $0–$40)

**Symptom this fixes:** Neck pain, upper back tension, tension headaches, "tech neck."

**The rule:** The top third of your screen should be roughly at eye level when you're sitting upright. If you're looking *down* at your laptop, you're grinding your cervical spine every workday.

### Cheapest fix that works: books

Stack hardcover books under your monitor until the top edge sits at eye level. Free. Ugly. Works.

### The $25 fix: a monitor riser

A simple wooden or steel monitor stand. Look for one **4–6 inches tall** with a shelf underneath (useful for reclaiming desk space). We recommend the [Simple Wooden Monitor Riser — affiliate link placeholder].

### The $80 fix: a monitor arm

If you have a heavy monitor or want to reclaim serious desk space, a VESA monitor arm is worth every dollar. It lets you dial in height *and* distance precisely, and moves out of the way when you don't need it. Recommended: [Standard VESA Monitor Arm — affiliate link placeholder].

### Laptop users

If you're on a laptop, **you cannot have both correct screen height and correct keyboard height with one device.** Physics. Choose one:

- **Best:** Laptop on a riser (screen at eye level) + external keyboard and mouse.
- **Acceptable:** External monitor at eye level, laptop closed or as second screen below.
- **Wrong:** Laptop on your lap, or on the desk with nothing under it, for 8 hours a day. This is the single biggest cause of "sudden onset" WFH neck pain.

## Fix #2: Elbows at 90° (cost: $0–$150)

**Symptom this fixes:** Shoulder tension, wrist pain, "mouse arm," early tingling in fingers.

**The rule:** When you're typing, your upper arms should hang relaxed and your forearms should be roughly parallel to the floor — elbows at about 90°.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: **standard desks are 29–30 inches tall**, and that is too tall for the average adult using a keyboard. Furniture makers standardized around writing height (when you rest your forearm on the desk), not typing height (when your hands hover above it).

### Diagnosing the problem

Sit up straight in your chair. Let your arms hang relaxed at your sides, then bend your elbows to 90°. **Where do your hands land?** That's where your keyboard needs to be. If your desk surface is above that point, you have a problem.

### Fix, in order of cost

1. **Raise your chair, use a footrest ($0–$30).** Cheapest fix. Crank the chair until your elbows hit 90° at the desk. If your feet dangle, put a box, a stack of books, or a proper footrest under them.
2. **Under-desk keyboard tray ($60–$120).** Mounts to the underside of your desk, drops your keyboard 3–5 inches below the desk surface. This is the single most underrated ergonomic purchase.
3. **Adjustable-height desk ($200–$600).** Overkill for most people, but if you're going to buy one anyway, get one that actually goes low enough (many "standing desks" don't drop below 28 inches when seated — check the spec).

### About that split keyboard

Split keyboards (Kinesis, ZSA Moonlander, Keychron Q11) are genuinely good if you type all day, but they are a **level 2** intervention. Get your desk height right first. A split keyboard on a too-tall desk is still bad ergonomics — you've just made it look expensive.

## Fix #3: Movement (cost: $0)

**Symptom this fixes:** Everything the first two fixes don't.

Here is the finding that surprised us most: in the long-time remote workers we talked to who stayed pain-free, **nobody had a perfect setup**. What they all had was a **movement habit**.

The human body is not designed to hold any single posture for hours — not even the "correct" one. A perfect ergonomic setup that you sit still in for 8 hours will still hurt you.

### The rule that actually works: 30/30/30

- Every **30 minutes**: stand up. Even just for 20 seconds.
- Every **30 minutes of standing**: sit back down. (Yes, standing all day is also bad. Standing desks are not a cheat code.)
- Every **30 minutes of screen work**: look at something at least 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This is the "20/20/20 rule" for eye strain, and it works.

### How to actually do it

Willpower does not scale. Automate the reminder:

- **Free:** Any Pomodoro timer app. We like the built-in Focus timer in macOS/iOS, or the free web app [tomato-timer.com](https://tomato-timer.com).
- **Free:** A physical kitchen timer. Underrated. It doesn't nag you inside the tool you're already working in.
- **$5:** A basic countdown timer on your phone lock screen.

We are deliberately not recommending an app that sends you 40 notifications a day. If your work is the kind that requires deep focus, notification-based reminders will backfire — you'll dismiss them, then dismiss them harder, then turn them off.

## Fix #4: The chair (cost: $150–$1,500)

Notice this is #4, not #1. **A great chair does not save you from bad monitor height, bad desk height, or not moving.** But once those are handled, a chair upgrade compounds.

### What actually matters in a chair

Ignore the marketing. Only four things determine whether a chair works for you:

1. **Seat height adjustment range.** Must go low enough that your feet sit flat on the floor at your correct desk height. Most cheap chairs bottom out at 17 inches — too tall for anyone under about 5'6".
2. **Seat depth.** From backrest to front edge. Too deep = the front edge cuts into the back of your knees. Look for a chair with adjustable seat depth if you are shorter than 5'8" or taller than 6'2".
3. **Lumbar support that hits your actual lumbar.** Adjustable height on the lumbar cushion is worth paying for. Fixed lumbar that's positioned for a 6-foot person is useless if you're 5'4".
4. **Adjustable armrests.** They need to go low enough to support your arms at your correct typing height. Fixed armrests are almost always wrong.

### Price tiers

- **$150–$300 — Entry.** Buy used. A used Herman Miller Aeron or Steelcase Leap in decent condition on Facebook Marketplace is a better chair than any new $300 chair. This is one of the strongest used-market plays in consumer goods. [Used Aeron search — affiliate link placeholder]
- **$400–$800 — Mid.** New chairs from Autonomous, Branch, FlexiSpot. Fine. Not magic. [Branch Ergonomic Chair — affiliate link placeholder]
- **$1,000+ — Premium.** Herman Miller, Steelcase, Humanscale new. Excellent chairs, but you are paying a large premium over buying the same chair used. Only makes sense if you value warranty and don't want to deal with a used-market purchase.

### The uncomfortable answer nobody gives

If you sit correctly, move every 30 minutes, and have a $200 used chair — you will do better than 90% of remote workers with $1,500 new chairs who sit still for 4 hours at a stretch.

## Fix #5: Peripherals (cost: $30–$300)

The order to buy these in, once the above is handled:

1. **External keyboard** (if you're on a laptop). Almost anything is better than a laptop keyboard for 8 hours a day. Budget pick: [Logitech K380 — affiliate link placeholder]. Enthusiast pick: [Keychron K2 — affiliate link placeholder].
2. **Vertical mouse.** If you have any wrist pain, a vertical mouse is often a bigger fix than a fancy keyboard. Rotates your forearm to a neutral position. Recommended: [Logitech MX Vertical — affiliate link placeholder].
3. **Second monitor.** Not strictly ergonomic, but reduces neck-craning if you spend all day switching tabs. Match the height of your primary monitor.
4. **Wrist rest.** Only if you rest your wrists while typing (you shouldn't, ideally — hover). Foam is usually more comfortable than gel long-term.

## What NOT to buy (or at least, not yet)

We're going to lose some affiliate commission by writing this section. That's fine.

- **Balance ball chairs.** Marketed as ergonomic. They are not. Your core cannot stabilize you for 8 hours; you end up slouching worse than on a normal chair.
- **Kneeling chairs.** Same problem. Fine for 30 minutes as posture variety. Terrible as your primary chair.
- **"Ergonomic" gel wrist rests that raise your wrist above the keyboard.** Actively bad — they extend your wrist backward, which is the position that causes carpal tunnel symptoms.
- **Standing desk without an anti-fatigue mat.** Standing on a hard floor for hours will destroy your feet and knees. If you buy a standing desk, buy a mat with it.
- **Blue-light glasses.** The evidence for these is weak. If you have eye strain, fix your lighting and take the 20/20/20 breaks. Save the money.

## When to see an actual professional

We are not doctors, and no blog post replaces one. See a physical therapist if:

- Pain has persisted more than **2 weeks** after fixing your setup.
- You have any **numbness or tingling** in your fingers (possible nerve involvement).
- Any pain wakes you up at night.
- You have shooting pain rather than dull ache.

A single PT session for an assessment is often $150–$250 and can save you thousands of dollars in wrong ergonomic purchases plus years of pain. This is the single best "ergonomic purchase" most people never consider.
