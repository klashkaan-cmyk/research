# The 4-Minute WFH Microbreak Routine: What To Actually Do When You Stand Up

**TL;DR:** Every ergonomics guide says "take breaks." Nobody says *what to do during them*. Standing up to scroll on your phone doesn't reset anything. Below: a 4-minute routine you can run every 45–60 minutes that undoes what sitting does to the specific tissues that hurt most in WFH.

## Why "take breaks" is bad advice on its own

The recommendation to break every 45–60 minutes is real. But breaks only work if they load the tissues that sitting *unloaded* and unload the tissues that sitting *loaded*. Standing up and walking to the kitchen for coffee gets you 30% of the benefit. Standing up and doing four specific movements gets you 90%. Same time cost.

The tissues that need help after 45+ minutes of sitting:

- **Hip flexors** — shortened by sustained hip flexion. Feel like concrete when you finally stand.
- **Glutes** — turned off by sitting on them. Contribute to lower-back pain because they stop supporting the pelvis.
- **Thoracic spine** — stuck in flexion from screen posture. Blame for upper back and neck tightness.
- **Deep neck flexors** — inhibited by forward-head posture. Their weakness is why "just sit up straight" doesn't hold for 5 minutes.
- **Wrists/forearms** — sustained low-grade contraction. Not injured, just fatigued.

Four minutes, one move per tissue, run every 45–60 minutes. That's the whole protocol.

## The routine (4 minutes, no gear)

### Minute 1: Hip flexor + glute reset

**Half-kneeling hip flexor stretch — 30 seconds each side.**
Kneel on one knee (a folded towel under the knee if your floor is hard). Front foot flat, front knee at 90°. Tuck your pelvis under (imagine trying to point your beltline UP toward your chin). Squeeze the glute on the down-leg side. You should feel a stretch in the front of the hip of the down leg.

- **Do:** pelvic tuck first, THEN a slight forward lean if you need more. Squeeze the glute continuously — that's what makes this a *reset*, not just a stretch.
- **Don't:** arch your lower back and lean forward. That stretches the wrong stuff and can compress the lumbar spine.

**Why:** hip flexors shortened + glutes turned off is the #1 postural mess from sitting. This move addresses both in one shape.

### Minute 2: Thoracic extension

**Wall or doorway thoracic extension — 30 seconds.**
Stand facing away from a wall, heels 6–8 inches from the wall. Place your hands behind your head, elbows wide. Now push your upper back — specifically the area between your shoulder blades — into the wall. Keep the lower ribs DOWN (don't just arch your low back to fake it).

Alternative if you have a chair with a low back: sit in the chair, hands behind head, lean back so your mid-back drapes over the top of the backrest. Same idea.

- **Do:** think "open the chest by extending the upper back," not "arch the low back."
- **Don't:** flare the ribs upward. That's lumbar extension, not thoracic. If your lower back does the work, your mid-back gets nothing.

**Why:** all-day forward posture stiffens the thoracic spine into flexion. Extension breaks that up. This is the single move that most reliably relieves upper-back tension.

### Minute 3: Chin tuck + wall angel

**Chin tucks — 5 slow reps.**
Sitting or standing, pull your chin STRAIGHT back (not down — think "double chin") until you feel the back of your neck lengthen. Hold 3 seconds. Release. Repeat 5 times.

**Wall angels — 10 slow reps.**
Stand with your back against a wall, feet 6 inches from the wall, low back flat against wall. Arms up in a "goal post" (elbows at shoulder height, 90° bend, backs of hands against wall). Slowly slide arms up toward a Y overhead while keeping wrists and elbows on the wall. Slide back down.

- **Do:** move slowly. The value is in the range you actually keep contact with the wall — not the range you fake by pulling off it.
- **Don't:** shrug during the slide. Keep shoulders pulled down.

**Why:** chin tucks re-engage the deep neck flexors that forward-head posture inhibits. Wall angels reset shoulder blade position and upper-back rotation.

### Minute 4: Wrist + forearm reset

**Prayer stretch — 20 seconds.**
Palms together in front of your chest ("prayer" position), fingertips up. Lower the hands (elbows lift) until you feel a stretch across the palm-side of the wrist and forearm. Hold.

**Reverse prayer or wrist flexor stretch — 20 seconds.**
Extend one arm forward, palm up, fingers pointing down. Use the other hand to gently pull fingertips toward you. Switch sides.

**Finger extensions — 10 reps.**
Make a loose fist, then spread fingers wide. Do 10.

**Why:** typing loads finger flexors and pronators. These moves stretch the flexors and re-engage extensors. Not injury prevention exactly — fatigue prevention.

## Break frequency

- **Every 45–60 minutes if you're mostly sitting.**
- **Every 60–90 minutes if you're alternating sit/stand.** Alternation itself does some of the work.
- **Skip the routine if you take a real walk (5+ minutes).** Walking covers most bases. Walking + the wrist reset (minute 4 only) covers all of them.

Set a repeating timer. Habit stacks: end-of-Zoom-meeting, task completion, hourly chime. Any consistent trigger works.

## Micro-versions (when you can't do the full 4 minutes)

Real life: meetings run long, deep-work sessions run 90+ minutes, you skip breaks. Rather than skip entirely, do micro-versions.

- **30-second version:** just Minute 1 (hip flexor + glute). This one gives the biggest single-move return.
- **60-second version:** Minute 1 + Minute 2 (thoracic extension). Covers the whole postural chain.
- **At-desk version:** chin tucks (5 reps), shoulder rolls (5 back), and finger spreads (10). No standing required. Take when you literally can't leave the chair.

Any version beats nothing. Don't let perfect ruin adequate.

## What NOT to do during microbreaks

- **Scroll on your phone.** Same posture as the one you're supposed to be breaking. Zero benefit.
- **Sit in a different chair.** Still sitting. Position the hip flexors don't care about.
- **"Stretch harder" to compensate for skipped breaks.** Doesn't work. Frequency matters more than intensity.
- **Overhead-only stretches like the classic "reach up and yawn."** Feels satisfying but only stretches lats. Missing hip flexors, glutes, thoracic spine — the actually-important tissues.
- **Foam rolling every break.** Great to do once a day. Overkill and time-eating every hour.

## What NOT to buy

Almost nothing. This is a routine, not a product category. If you want structure, one purchase is defensible:

- **A simple repeating timer** (physical desk timer or a break app like Stretchly, Time Out, Awareness). $0–$25. Removes willpower from the equation.

You do not need "stretching apps," "posture correctors," or "ergonomic reminder wearables." All negatively surveyed in the peer-reviewed literature for the outcomes that matter.

## The 2-week test

Track how your upper back, neck, and hips feel by end of day. If you consistently do the routine 6–8 times per workday for 2 weeks:

- **Upper-back tightness** should reduce noticeably. This is the fastest-changing signal.
- **Hip stiffness on standing up** should reduce.
- **Neck strain** takes longer (4–6 weeks) — deep neck flexors are slow to strengthen.

If two weeks in you feel no change, either you're not actually doing it consistently OR your pain source isn't postural (see a clinician).

## Bottom line

"Take breaks" is meaningless advice. This is the 4-minute version of what breaks are supposed to do. No gear, no gym, no cost. Set a timer, run it every 45–60 minutes, and the ergonomics you paid for at your desk actually get to do their job.
