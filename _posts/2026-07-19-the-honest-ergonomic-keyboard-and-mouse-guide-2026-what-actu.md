If you have wrist pain from WFH typing, the ergonomic gear industry has a lot of solutions to sell you. Most of them don't work. A few genuinely do, and the science on which is which has been settled for over a decade.

This is the honest version — what actually reduces wrist pain, backed by biomechanics research, ranked by evidence quality.

## Why "normal" keyboards and mice hurt in the first place

The pain isn't random. Standard (rectangular, flat) keyboards and mice force your wrists and forearms into three specific unnatural positions, and holding those positions 8 hours a day is what causes the injury.

**1. Ulnar deviation.** When you place both hands on a standard keyboard, your wrists bend outward — pinkies further from the centerline than thumbs. Rempel et al. (2007) and subsequent replications show ulnar deviation above 15° sustained for hours is a primary risk factor for carpal tunnel syndrome. Standard keyboards force 20–30° of deviation for most adult hand widths.

**2. Forearm pronation.** Palms-down (pronated) forearms compress the median nerve and radial artery over hours. Your forearm's neutral position is a handshake angle — roughly 45° tilted, thumb slightly up. Every second you're on a flat mouse or keyboard, you're locked in maximum pronation.

**3. Wrist extension.** When you rest your palms on the desk and reach up to keys, your wrist bends backward. Any wrist extension over 15° sustained is associated with elevated median-nerve pressure. Most laptop-only setups produce 25–40° of extension.

The three real ergonomic interventions target these three positions directly. Everything else is marketing.

## What actually helps (evidence-graded)

### Strong evidence (do this if wrist pain is real)

**Split keyboard.** Separating the two halves so each hand types at shoulder width kills ulnar deviation. Multiple RCTs (van Tulder et al. Cochrane review; NIOSH ergonomic keyboard studies) show wrist symptom reduction in the 30–50% range after 8+ weeks of adaptation. This is the single biggest evidence-backed intervention available.

**Tenting (angled keyboard halves).** Raising the inner edges of a split keyboard so each half tilts 10–30° toward the vertical reduces forearm pronation. Combined with split, this is the closest a keyboard gets to your forearm's neutral position.

**Vertical or trackball mouse.** Rotating your hand to a handshake position (vertical mouse) or eliminating hand movement entirely (trackball) kills pronation and reduces the small-muscle load in the forearm. Multiple observational studies show symptom reduction; RCT evidence is weaker but consistent.

### Moderate evidence

**Ortholinear layout (keys in a grid, not staggered).** Reduces finger travel and awkward reaches. Evidence is mostly typing-speed and comfort self-reports, not injury RCTs. Reasonable to try if you're already going split.

**Programmable layers (moving common keys — arrows, brackets, backspace — onto home-row layers).** Reduces total finger travel by 20–40% for coders. Solid biomechanical rationale, weak clinical evidence.

### Weak or no evidence (don't waste money)

**Gel wrist rests.** Actually harmful for most people — they encourage resting the wrist while typing, which compresses the carpal tunnel. Only useful as a *palm* rest between typing bursts.

**"Ergonomic" gaming keyboards with a slight curve.** The 4–8° curve on a Logitech G815 or similar is cosmetic. It doesn't move your wrists out of ulnar deviation meaningfully.

**Negative-tilt keyboard trays.** Popular in the 90s. Modern evidence is mixed at best; some studies show they help, others show they shift load to the shoulder. Not worth $200.

**Copper-infused wrist braces sold on Instagram.** No mechanism. No evidence. Skip.

## Keyboards worth buying

I'm not listing 15 keyboards. Here are the five that are actually worth the money, and who each fits.

**Kinesis Advantage 360 (~$449).** The full-fat option. Split, tented, ortholinear, concave key wells that match finger arc length, thumb clusters that move common keys off your pinkies. 20+ years of iteration behind it. This is the "I want the maximum evidence-backed intervention and money isn't the constraint" answer. Steep learning curve — typing speed drops 40–60% for the first two weeks, back to baseline by week 4–6 for most people.

**ZSA Voyager (~$365).** Low-profile split, tent-able with the optional tenting kit, ortholinear, programmable via a good browser-based configurator. Lighter learning curve than the Kinesis because it's flatter. My default recommendation for people who want the split/tent/ortho stack without the concave key wells.

**Keychron Q11 (~$220).** Split (via a hinge), traditional key layout (staggered, not ortholinear). Good gateway keyboard — gets you most of the split benefit without the ortholinear relearn. Solid build.

**Logitech Ergo K860 (~$130).** Not truly split (the halves are fused), but has a modest tent and a wave shape that reduces ulnar deviation partially. The best "office-appropriate, no learning curve" option. Real benefit vs. flat rectangular keyboard, less than a true split.

**MoErgo Glove80 (~$399).** Similar tier to the Kinesis Advantage 360 — concave wells, thumb clusters, wireless. Lighter and more comfortable for smaller hands. Fanatical user base; supply is intermittent.

Discontinued but findable: **Microsoft Sculpt Ergonomic** (~$100 used). Not truly split, but a good curved partial-split at a fraction of the price. If your budget is tight and you want *some* ergonomic benefit without learning ortholinear, buy a used Sculpt on eBay.

## Mice worth buying

**Logitech MX Vertical (~$100).** 57° vertical tilt, comfortable grip, good sensor, works on all major OSes. Default recommendation if you're switching from a flat mouse.

**Anker Vertical Mouse (~$25).** Same concept, ~1/4 the price, 90% as good. If budget is the constraint or you want to test whether vertical helps you before committing, this is the low-risk trial.

**Logitech MX Ergo Trackball (~$100).** Thumb-operated trackball on an angled base. Eliminates arm movement (which is the actual ergonomic win). Learning curve is 3–5 days, not weeks. The single best switch I've made for my own wrist pain.

**Kensington Expert Mouse Trackball (~$85).** Larger, finger-operated ball. Some people find this more comfortable than thumb trackballs; it depends on which of your muscles are already irritated. Try if the MX Ergo doesn't fit.

**Handshoe Mouse (~$100–200).** Custom-molded to your hand size. Overkill for most, essential for people with specific hand-shape issues. Get it if a therapist recommends it, otherwise start with the MX Vertical.

## The switching-cost problem (why most people give up)

The single biggest reason people quit ergonomic keyboards is that they don't budget for the productivity dip.

Expect: 40–60% typing speed loss on day one with a true split ortholinear. 30% back by day 3–4. 90% back by week 3. 100%+ (yes, faster than baseline) by month 2–3 for most people.

If you're going to buy a Kinesis Advantage 360 and use it for a week during a launch, you'll fail. Buy it before a lighter workload. Give it a month.

Vertical mice: much smaller adaptation. 2–3 days.

Trackballs: 3–5 days, then you'll wonder why you ever moved a mouse across a desk.
