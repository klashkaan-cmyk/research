# Methodology and Editorial Policy

*Last updated 2026-07-19 · WFH Ergonomics Research · info@boogiemanmarketing.com*

This page exists so you can decide, before trusting any of our buyer's guides, whether our recommendations are worth trusting. We publish evidence-cited product guides for remote knowledge workers. This page tells you exactly how we grade evidence, which sources we treat as authoritative, how we handle disclosure, and what we will not do — even for money.

---

## What we cover

- Ergonomic gear for people who work at a desk for most of their day
- Setup guides tied to peer-reviewed and standards-body evidence
- Honest buyer's guides across price bands, including budget picks
- Explicit "avoid at any price" lists when the evidence supports it

## What we do not cover

- Anything we cannot cite. If a claim would require a source we do not have, we cut the claim.
- Medical treatment. We reference clinical literature to inform gear recommendations. We are not clinicians. Persistent pain warrants a physical therapist or physician, not a chair.
- Products we cannot describe honestly. If a company sends a review unit with strings attached, we decline. If we cannot describe a real weakness of a product, we do not review it.

---

## Evidence grading scale

We label the underlying evidence for each recommendation with one of four labels. These map roughly to how confident we are that following the recommendation will produce the claimed benefit.

**Strong.** Multiple randomized or well-controlled studies, consensus standards from a body like ANSI, IES, ISO, BIFMA, or a Cochrane-grade systematic review point the same direction. Example: seat height adjustability reducing lower-extremity discomfort in seated computer work.

**Moderate.** Several observational studies, one strong RCT, or a standard from a credible body without independent replication yet. Example: adjustable lumbar support reducing self-reported low-back discomfort.

**Weak.** Physiologically plausible mechanism plus at least one small study or field report, but not enough evidence to promise a benefit. Example: bias lighting behind a monitor reducing eye fatigue.

**Preference.** No evidence advantage — this is a comfort or aesthetic choice, and we say so. Example: mesh vs foam seat pans in the $250–$500 chair range.

If a recommendation carries no label in a guide, treat it as at least "moderate" — we will retro-label it on the next update.

## Sources we treat as authoritative

**Standards bodies.**
- ANSI/HFES 100-2007 — Human Factors Engineering of Computer Workstations
- BIFMA G1-2013 — Ergonomics Guideline for Furniture Used in Office Work Spaces
- IES RP-1 — Recommended Practice for Lighting Office Spaces
- EN 12464-1 — Indoor workplace lighting standard
- ISO 9241 series — Ergonomics of human-system interaction
- OSHA Computer Workstations eTool

**Systematic reviews and clinical journals.**
- Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews
- American Journal of Ophthalmology / AAO clinical guidance
- Applied Ergonomics, Human Factors, Ergonomics (journals)
- Journal of Electromyography and Kinesiology
- Optometry and Vision Science

**Government and public health sources.**
- NIOSH (National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health)
- CDC workplace health guidance
- OSHA guidance and standards

We prefer these over vendor marketing, over YouTube reviews of any kind, and over Reddit consensus. When Reddit consensus and peer-reviewed evidence disagree, we say so and cite the evidence.

## Sources we treat with skepticism

- Vendor-funded studies without independent replication
- YouTube reviews of any kind (this includes ours if we ever make them)
- "Chiropractic evidence" for spinal loading claims — we prefer orthopedic or occupational health sources
- Blog posts that cite other blog posts
- Affiliate-review aggregators (they are optimizing for click-through, not accuracy)

## Product recommendations

**How we pick products for a guide.** We start with the four to six specifications that the evidence actually supports as ergonomically meaningful for that product category. We name real products at price bands, disclose the trade-offs of each, and include an explicit "would not buy at any price" list when the evidence justifies it.

**We do not accept review units** as of publish date. If we begin accepting them, we will disclose per-review and label those reviews explicitly. Sending an unsolicited review unit does not obligate us to review it or return it.

**We include budget picks.** A guide that only recommends $500+ products has failed most readers. Every price band gets an honest pick, including the "acceptable, not aspirational" tier.

**We name products we would avoid.** Most product review sites soft-pedal the negative. We are direct about which products or product categories we consider design defects for the use case, and we say why. This is the section that makes the guides useful.

**We say when we do not know.** If a product is new or under-reviewed, we say so and note we cannot recommend or reject it yet.

## Affiliate and monetization policy

**Current status: no active affiliate relationships as of 2026-07-19.**

If we add affiliate relationships in the future:

1. We will disclose them on each affected post, at the top and in every product mention.
2. We will not change recommendations to favor higher-commission products. If we do, we will have failed and the site is worthless.
3. We will preserve the "would not buy" lists even for products from partner programs.
4. We will make the disclosure easy to find on the homepage and in a persistent site footer, not buried on this page.
5. We will publish a change log the first time affiliate relationships go live so returning readers can audit whether recommendations shifted.

**We will never** run sponsored posts, native advertising, or "collaborations" that involve editorial input from a vendor. If we cannot write about a product freely, we will not write about it.

**Reader contact CTA** is a real conversion primitive. If you have a question, a product suggestion, or a correction, email info@boogiemanmarketing.com — a real human reads every message.

## Corrections policy

We publish corrections openly. If a reader points out an error, or we find one:

1. We fix the post inline.
2. We add a dated correction note at the top of the affected post.
3. If the correction changes a recommendation materially (from "buy" to "avoid," or vice versa), we say so explicitly.

We would rather publish a correction than defend a wrong claim.

---

## What we cite and what we do not

**We cite** peer-reviewed studies, standards documents, systematic reviews, government occupational health sources, and identifiable clinical guidance.

**We do not cite** other blog posts, unnamed "experts," or "studies have shown" without a specific study. If a claim requires that kind of hedging, we cut the claim.

**We link** to primary sources when they are freely accessible. When a source is paywalled, we cite it by author-year and journal so a reader can find it through their library or a preprint server.
